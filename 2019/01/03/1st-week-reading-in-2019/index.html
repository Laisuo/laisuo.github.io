Science News

  • Each year, Science’s editors and writers highlight a top research achievement as their Breakthrough of the Year. For 2018, the honor goes to tracking development in exquisite detail, cell by cell. But there a lot of other advances to talk about, from the debut of an RNA interference drug to the first detection of a neutrino from another galaxy. Check them all out in this video run-down.

Source: Science Magazine&apos;s 2018 Breakthrough of the Year by ScienceMagazine

Here is a link to details of these Breakthroughs (and breakdowns)

  • Our favorite science news stories of 2018: Every year, Science publishes hundreds of news stories, both online and in our weekly magazine. And whereas many of these highlight huge advances in research (some of which get a nod in our breakthroughs of the year), a lot are simply cool stories that resonated with us, our readers, or both. And that’s what this list focuses on—some of our coolest and most popular online news stories of the year. It’s an eclectic mix, and you’re sure to find at least a few you’ll want to read—or read all over again.

Nature Breifing

  • What to expect in 2019: science in the new year: Gene-editing, open access and a biosafety rethink are set to shape research.
    • Polar projects, Big bucks (China might become the biggest research country), Human origins, Collider crunch, Gene-editing fallout, Planning for Plan S, Biosafety bible, Climate tinkering, High hopes, Cosmic signals (The world’s largest radio telescope from China)

  • The Top 10 Science Papers of 2018 Are an Apocalyptic Nightmare: It’s Top 10 season at Slate, with all the usual enumerations. We’ve got lists of the year’s 10 best books, 10 best jazz albums, 10 best TV shows, 10 best movies, 10 best albums that might or might not be jazz, and so forth. Today, I’d like to call attention to a different sort of list, a profoundly disturbing and depressing one. I mean, of course, it’s time to look at the top 10 scientific papers of 2018.

  • Nature’s 10: Ten people who mattered this year.
    1. YUAN CAO: Graphene wrangler. A PhD student coaxed superconductivity from sheets of atom-thick carbon.
    2. VIVIANE SLON: Humanity’s historian. A palaeogeneticist discovered a remarkable ancient hybrid hominin: half Neanderthal, half Denisovan.
    3. HE JIANKUI: CRISPR rogue. A scientist’s claim to have created gene-edited babies generated international furore.
    4. JESS WADE: Diversity champion. A physicist wrote hundreds of Wikipedia pages to boost the profiles of scientists from under-represented groups.
    5. VALÉRIE MASSON-DELMOTTE: Earth monitor. A climatologist was a driving force behind the IPCC’s stark report on global warming.
    6. ANTHONY BROWN: Star mapper. Working behind the scenes, an astronomer coordinated the release of Gaia’s long-awaited bounty of Milky Way data.
    7. BEE YIN YEO: Force for the environment. Malaysia’s new science and environment minister became a strong voice against plastic pollution.
    8. BARBARA RAE-VENTER: DNA detective. A genealogist helped to identify a serial killer and paved the way for DNA to play a larger part in solving crimes.
    9. ROBERT-JAN SMITS: Open-access leader. A bureaucrat launched a drive to transform science publishing.
    10. MAKOTO YOSHIKAWA: Asteroid hunter. An astronomer leads a mission to collect samples from an asteroid.
