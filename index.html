Martin Winter

(1/29/2018 9:08:52 AM )
Review: Pre-Lithiation Strategies for Rechargeable Energy Storage Technologies: Concepts, Promises and Challenges
* In order to compensate the loss of active lithium, mostly from the first cycle due to SEI formation, various “pre-lithiation” methods have been developed that result in an increased reversible capacity and, consequently, in a higher gravimetric energy or volumetric energy densities. This “pre-lithiation” can be conducted to both negative electrode and positive electrode. It is important to note that the degree of pre-lithiation has to be adjusted with a high accuracy. Otherwise, an over-lithiation could lead to an excess of lithium, thus lithium metal plating on top of the negative electrodes surface, resulting in safety hazards during operation.
* In this review paper, different “Pre-lithiation” techniques for Negative electrode, positive electrode in LIB are introduced and compared. The concept of “pre-lithiation” is also extended and applied in other applications like Lithium-Ion Capacitors, Li-Ion/Sulphur Batteries, and Rechargeable Oxygen Batteries.

Arumugam Manthiram

Joule: Designing Lithium-Sulfur Cells with Practically Necessary Parameters. Professor Manthiram and Dr. Chung present a coaxial-graphene-coated cottoncarbon material as an excellent cathode substrate that simultaneously satisfies the demand for high sulfur loading, high sulfur content, low electrolyte amount, and superior electrochemical efficiency and stability. Benefiting from a high-loading cathode configuration, the cells attain areal capacity and energy density of 8 and 7 times higher, respectively, than those obtained with a commercial LiCoO2 cathode. The coaxial-graphene-coated cotton-carbon cathode demonstrates a promising direction for the establishment of practical lithium-sulfur battery technology.

Batteries and Energy Storage

Factors Limiting \(Li^+\) Charge Transfer Kinetics in Li-Ion Batteries: When \(Li^+\) moves from one electrode to another during charge or discharge, it has to go through multiple steps. The interesting question is that which step is the rate-limited step.
* The Li+ charge transfer process is defined as the process of turning a solvated Li+ in the electrolyte into Li in the electrode by accepting an electron from the electrode. The rate-limited step depends on the type of a system. Mostly, the limited step is either the desovation of the solvated \(Li^+\) in the liquid electrolyte or the step of transport of \(Li^+\) in the preformed solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) on electrodes.
* When the SEI is very conductive and of low activation energy for \(Li^+\) conduction, such as LLT and Ohara glass, the \(Li^+\) charge transfer kinetics is dominated by the de-solvation process. * In the case when there is no SEI or CEI, or insignificant SEI/CEI, such as with the LTO/electrolyte inteface or LFP/electrolyte interface, the \(Li^+\) charge transfer kinetics is dominated by de-solvation or by the Li transport in the electrode. * As for the effect of additive, the LiBOB additive leads to a reduced \(R_{ct}\) and \(E_a\) at the NCA cathode, but results in much increased \(R_{ct}\) and \(E_a\) at the graphite anode. The LiFSI additive amongst those studied in this work provides the best overall benefits in terms of \(Li^+\) charge transfer kinetic improvement.

Study on Potassium Doped Modification of \(Li_{1.2}Ni_{0.13}Co_{0.13}Mn_{0.54}O_2\) Materials Synthesized by Novel Method for Lithium Ion Battery: Reasonable \(K^+\) doping improves the rate and cycling performance of Li-rich ternary cathode material \(Li_{1.2}Ni_{0.13}Co_{0.13}Mn_{0.54}O_2\) owing to the enhancement of the structural stability and the enlargement of the Li slab space and improvement of the diffusion of Li ions in the bulk lattice.

Material Characterization Technique_XPS

Posted on 2018-01-11 | In Reading |

Principle

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is based on the photoelectric effect that proposed by Einstein in 1905. When X-rays are projected to the surface of a sample, atomes in the sample are ionized and the kinetic energies of the emitted photoelectrons are measured. In this way, the binding energy of the electron can be calculated.[1] \[E_{binding}=E_{photon}-(E_{kinetic}+\theta)\]
where \(\theta\) is work function of the studied element.
Due to the small mean free path of the emitted electrons in solides (~ 10 nm), XPS is only surface sensitive. But combining with ion sputting, XPS can give depth profiles of a sample.
