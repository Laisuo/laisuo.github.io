How Britain got to 50% low-carbon power

Why scientists need to do more about research fraud

There is a spectrum of wrongdoing in science, from selecting the one result out of many that makes sense to you, up to deliberately faking figures in papers. And until there is a change in culture such that people are encouraged to say, “Guys, this isn’t right” – such as I might have been a decade ago – there will continue to be high-profile cases of seriously damaging scientific fraud.

News from Science

Light pollution may promote the spread of West Nile virus

West Nile is primarily a bird virus, but people occasionally get infected because some bird-biting mosquitoes dine on human blood as well. Human infections can cause fevers, body aches, rashes, diarrhea, long-term fatigue, and, in some cases, inflammation of the brain and its membranes. In the United States, almost 2000 people have died from West Nile since its arrival.
Birds exposed to light can survive two more days compared to that in night, which increase the chance to transport the virus to mosquitoes. Clinton Francis, an ecologist at the California Polytechnic State University in San Luis Obispo, agrees. “The ways that we influence sensory environments”—whether it’s with sound or light”—can have all sorts of unintended consequences,” he says.

After years of avoidance, Department of Energy joins quest to develop quantum computers

The U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) is joining the quest to develop quantum computers, devices that would exploit quantum mechanics to crack problems that overwhelm conventional computers. The new $40 million DOE effort is intended to spur the development of useful quantum computing algorithms for its work in chemistry, materials science, nuclear physics, and particle physics.

Mysterious radio bursts may be coming from neutron star orbiting black hole

Researchers found the first fast radio burst (FRB) 10 years ago, and have since found a handful of others. But the source of this enigmatic FRB is not clear. Recently, a team using the Arecibo radio telescope in Puerto Rico has discovered that the polarization of the bursts’ radio waves—the direction in which the waves vibrate—rotates in a fast and unpredictable way. This suggests, they report today in Nature, that the bursts come from somewhere very hot and with a very high magnetic field. Such conditions exist, they say, around a massive black hole (like the one above), and the short duration of the pulses suggests they come from something small, like a neutron star. A neutron star orbiting a black hole then?

Water ice cliffs on Mars

  • Some locations on Mars are known to have water ice just below the surface, but how much has remained unclear. Research recently found that the ice sheets extend from just below the surface to a depth of 100 meters or more and appear to contain distinct layers, which could preserve a record of Mars’ past climate. They might even be a useful source of water for future human exploration of the red planet.

Subject: Energy science and technology

Molecular understanding of polyelectrolyte binders that actively regulate ion transport in sulfur cathodes. (December 22, 2017)

Replacing PVDF binder by PEB-1 binder, the performance of Li-S cell is largely improved. To understand this improvement, the fundermental effect of binder is further studied and discussed.
Basically, the chemical constitution of a polymer binder should dictate wheter it is passive, active, or adaptive in in an electrode. But currently, it is not clear in literature, which casue obscure to rational design a polymer binder.
In this work, it shows polyelectrolyte binders actively exert their influence on the diffusive transport of various ionic species encountered in the cycling of sulfur electrodes. The effect includes both the facilitated transport of Li+ throughout the electrode and restricted active material diffusion so as to minimizing capacity fade at high sulfur loading.
More details of their discovery can be found in the final Discussion part of the article.

Coupling between oxygen redox and cation migration explains unusual electrochemistry in lithium-rich layered oxides

Besides O redox, transition metal migration also plays a role during charge/discharge process. This is improtant to understand many phenomena in Li-rich layered oxides, like open circuit voltage hysterisis.

Authours

Martin Winter

JES, Thermal Analysis of LiNi0. 4Co0. 2Mn0. 4O2/Mesocarbon Microbeads Cells and Electrodes: State-of-Charge and State-of-Health Influences on Reaction Kinetics

The thermal stability of 5 Ah pouch cells (NMC422/MCMB) is studied here. Cathode electrode and anode electrode were obtained at differet state of charge and state of health by disassembling, and their thermal stabilities were tested seperately. The general conclusion is that (1) The self heating onset temperature decrease with aging, and with incrasing of SOC, (2) Anode is more stable than cathode.
However, the conclusion here cannot be applied directly to full cell becasue the case is much more complex in full cell, including the effect of SEI, separator, and cathode-anode interaction. Thus, more studies need to be done to uncover the thermal stability of the cell.

JPS, New insights into pre-lithiation kinetics of graphite anodes via nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy

This article for the first time utilized NMR to monitor the apparent rate(s) of lithium metal dissolution and formation of the corresponding LiCx phases during the pre-lithiation process, yielding insights into apparent reaction kinetics of the pre-lithiation process. The dissolution rate of lithium metal decreases with the increasing of pre-lithiation degree.
