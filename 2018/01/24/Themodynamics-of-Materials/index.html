Lecture 1: Intro. and Laws of Classical Thermodynamics

Defination

Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is a branch of physics dealing with the transformation of heat into or from other forms of energy such as mechanical, magnetic, electrical etc. as well as the laws which govern such transformations. Gibbs added chemical work, so that thermodynamics is also applied to chemcial reactions etc.

System

Any two of the intensive variables of a system may be used to define its state. For example, we may fix the state of the water by saying that its heat capacity is \(C_1\) and its viscosity is \(v_1\) It is best to use as the defining intensive variable those which we can readily control such as Pressure (P) and temperature (T).

Thermodynamic Variables

  • Intensive variable: does not depend on size of system. Or, does not relate to (micro)structure of material. Like P, T
  • Extensive variable: depends on the size of the system. Or, relates to (micro)structure of material. Like V.
    In materials science we often differentiate between intrinsic properties and extrinsic properties. In a sense, thermodynamic state variables are intrinsic properties, independent of history.

Processes

Processes are path dependent. They change the state of a system. In thermodynamics, work and thermal energy are the main process we deal with.
Names of severial constraint processes: Isobaric(dP=1), Isothermal(dT=0), Isochoric(dV=0), Adiabatic(\(\delta q\)=0), Cyclic (all state variables \(\Delat\)=0). * Work: the line integral of the force times the displacement. Note that being a line integral it is by defination path dependent! \[W=\int F(x)·dx\]
The relationship between heat and work > The relation between heat (thermal energy) and work was suggested in 1798 by Count Rumford (aka Sir Benjamin Thomson, 1753-1814), who, during the boring of cannons at the Munich Arsenal, noticed that the heat, q, produced during the boring was roughly proportional to the work, w, performed during the boring.

Laws of Thermodynamics

Sometimes thermodynamics is summarized by stating its laws, which can be treated as experimentally determined facts of nature, or can be considered as axioms from which other thermodynamic relationships can be derived.

Zeroth Law (Temperature)

Statement: If A is in thermal equilibrium with C, B is in thermal equilibrium with C, then A is in thermal equilibrium with B.
This law is fundamental to our understanding of energy, heat, work and entropy and since it was stated after the first law, is was named the Zeroth Law.
Joseph Black was the founder of this Law. It was he who emphasized that there is a difference between Temperature and Heat content. He was a “father” of calorimetry and measured latent heats for the first time. James Watt (1736-1819) was his student.

First Law (Energy)

Statement: Energy is conserved. The first law not only states that the energy of the universe is conserved, but it also posits that any form of energy (such as electrical, magnetic, mechanical etc.) can be converted into other forms of energy.
\[dU=\delta Q-\delta W\] where U is internal energy, which is an important extensive thermodynamic state variable. This equation says heat can be transformed into work!

Second Law (Entropy)

Statement: The state of entropy of the entire universe, as an isolated system, will always increase over time.
This law allows us to make important predictions of the direction that a system will evolve with time during spontaneous processes, if other important caveats are taken into account. It gives time a direction!
> Comment: This law is the one that often gets attention in popular discussions of science. Indeed C. P. Snow once stated to the effect that a person not knowing the second law of thermodynamics was equivalent to a person who had never read a play by Shakespeare!

Third Law (Absolute 0 K)

Statement: when a system which is in equilibrium approaches the absolute zero in temperature, its entropy approaches zero as well. Another (and equivalent statement) of the law is sometimes called the unattainability law: a system can never be be taken to the absolute zero in temperature.
> Nernst Statement: \(\Delta S\) approaches 0 at T approaches 0
> Planck Statement: S approaches to 0 as T approaches 0
