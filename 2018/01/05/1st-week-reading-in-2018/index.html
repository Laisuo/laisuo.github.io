1st week reading in 2018

Posted on 2018-01-05 | In Reading | |

Science

What’s coming up in 2018 Link

The migration of scientists is not clear now due to the complex environment in U.S., European Union, and China. Here are some views about the development in several important aspects in the upcoming year.

Astronomy: A monster Black Hole that detected by by the Event Horizon Telescope team in April 2017 at the heart of our galaxy. After processing and correlated the data, they will give some interesting information.
Human origins: New cleus from ancient DNA. Besides obtained from bones, scientists announced that they had managed to pick it up from cave sediments, which will largely help scientists understand the evolution of humanbeings.
Public health: Epidemics make a return
Computer science: Quantum computing might coming soon with the hardworking of physicists in the last decades. The quantum computer relies on subtle phenomena like interference of quantum waves to perform calculations rather than traditional flipping ordinary bits, which gives it overwhelming power.
Biomedicine: Based on project All of Us study proposed by then-President Barack Obama in 2015, the U.S. National Institues of Health will hit the launch button on its most ambitious project ever that will take 10 years and cost $ 4 billion.
Biotechnology: A court ruling on gene editing. Public opposition to genetically modified organisms (GMOs), even though it does not insert foreign DNA from viruses or bacteria to alter a plant’s function.
Policy: The UK decision to leave European Union in 2019 has raised so many unanswered questions, and will still be policy variables in the upcoming year.
Funding: The European Commission’s next ambitious funding program for research and innovation will start taking shape this year. It is a 7-year program that follows Horizon 2020, which provide €77 billion between 2014 and 2020. However, the leaving of U.K. leads to a hole of €10 billion or €11 billion to the project.
Clinical research: RNA treatments move forward. The treatment using RNA is tricky becasue along with these delicate RNA payloads past the body’s defenses and into the desired tissue, there are some toxic effects from their delivery vehicles.
Public Health: Shots may treat HIV with an effect of 1-3 month, which takes much longer than pills that needs to be taken daily.
Space flight: China and India are planning to put down landers on the moon during the year (the Chang’e 4 missions and Chandrayaan-2 missions) and five companies are vying to deposit rovers there before the end of March to claim the Google Lunar X-Prize. Donald Trump also directed NASA to return U.S. astronauts to the moon, but it might be a little bit late.
Particle physics: Physicists are still in pursuit of new particles, although the last missing piece in the standard model of particles and forces, Higgs boson, has already been found in 2012.
Drug oversight: Food and Drug Administration (FDA) restricts stem cell therapies.
Politics: Many U.S. researchers have decided to take their activism to the next level by running for office.

Nature

What to expect in 2018: science in the new year

Moon missions, ancient genomes and a publishing showdown are set to shape research.

Ancient-genome studies could help to explain migration patterns in the Americas and genetic diversity among Native Americans

Cosmic data: The position and motion of more than one billion stars in the Milky Way will be discovered this year using Canadian Hydrogen Intensity Mapping Experiment (CHIME). The data collected will help to improve our understanding of the spiral structure of the Galaxy. Ancient Amercians: Ancient-genome studies in 2018 will try to explain when and how people expanded into Americas beginning around 15,000 years. This study might also help to explain the genetic diversity seen in todays’s Native American populations.
Scientific-unit revamp: Redefination of four units of measure, which are the ampere, the kilogram, the kelvin and the mole. The new defination will be based on exact values of fundamental constants, rather than on arbitrary or abstract definitions.
To the moon and beyond: U.S., China, and India are attempting to land rovers on the lunar surface.
Cancer’s bigger picture: Insights into the genes that regulate cancer could emerge this year as scientists pore over the first large-scale multiple-cancer sequencing effort of whole genomes.
Climate landmarks: Countries in 2015 Paris climate agreement will meet together to report their progress in reducing greenhouse-gas emissions.
Extreme imaging: New tools at X-ray free-electron laser (XFEL) facilities will enable scientists to image samples changing under high temperature and pressure.
Power play: Midterm electrions in U.S., and Brexit negotiation in U.K. will both determine the nations’ scientific collaboration in the upcoming year.
Space-industry battles: Up to 5 teams competing for the US$30 million Google Lunar XPrize.
Disease treatments: Bring gene-editing tools such as CRISPR-Cas9 to the clinc are growing.
Particle surfing: New method to accelerate particles will be tried. If successful, the technique could eventually lead to samller and cheaper colliders.
Open access: Who will blink first in the stand-off between German scientists and publishing giant Elsevier?

News in this week

  1. Power Prices go negative in Germany!
    Germany has spent $200 billion over the past two decades to promote cleaner sources of electricity. Now, consumers are actually paid to use power on occasion, as was the case over the weekend.

  2. Made in China: New and Potentially Lifesaving Drugs
    Three cancer cure drugs from China. One new drug promises to stop cancer from spreading to other organs. Another would treat blood cancer. A third would use the body’s immune system to kill tumors. If the three new drugs go through F.D.A., they can stand as proff of China’s growing ability to produce cutting-edge treatments.

The last time China produced a new drug for the global pharmaceutical industry was in the 1970s, when the Chinese scientist Tu Youyou was credited with the discovery of artemisinin after Mao Zedong asked her to come up with a cure for malaria.
