Nature

Nature Briefing

Revamped collider hunts for cracks in the fundamental theory of physics

The quest to explore the frontiers of physics will heat up next month in Japan, where the Belle II aims to chase down rare, promising hints of new phenomena that would extend the standard model — a remarkably successful but incomplete physics theory that describes matter and forces. The competition between Belle II and LHCb will move forward the developement of fundermental physics.

China enters the battle for AI talent

China government and companys have put many effort to try increase their ability in AI field. The ambition is to become a global leader in 2030. But it requires a large, highly skilled workforce. Many talent young Chinese students prefer to work for big companies in US, like Google and Facebook. Chinese companies are try to attract them by offering high salary and good working conditions. However, it takes time. Currently, China is good a leaner but not good innoviator.

East China Sea oil tanker disaster: what it means for the environment

The Sanchi, carrying 136,000 tonnes of light crude oil from Iran, had been in flames since colliding with the CF Crystal, a Hong Kong-registered bulk freighter, on 6 January. It was headed to South Korea to deliver its cargo with a crew of 32 – 30 Iranians and two Bangladeshis. The leakage of oil would have tremendous effect of the environment of nearby sea.

Artificial intelligence beats human readers

Alibaba has developed an artificial intelligence model that scored better than humans in a Stanford University reading and comprehension test. Alibaba Group Holding Ltd. put its deep neural network model through its paces last week, asking the AI to provide exact answers to more than 100,000 questions comprising a quiz that’s considered one of the world’s most authoritative machine-reading gauges. The model developed by Alibaba’s Institute of Data Science of Technologies scored 82.44, edging past the 82.304 that rival humans achieved.

The Fields Medal should return to its roots

2018 is a Fields Medal year! Like Olympic medals and World Cup trophies, the best-known prizes in mathematics come around only every four years (Beginning in 1936).
Notably, the Nobel is typically given to senior figures, often decades after the contribution being honoured. By contrast, Fields medallists are at an age at which, in most sciences, a promising career would just be taking off.

What a US government shutdown would mean for science

I cannot believe it. The congress failed to negotiate a spending plan for the full 2018 budget year. If lawmakers cannot make a plan with the agreement from Congress and the White House, it would be forced to cease many government services and shutter facilities.

European Union moves to strengthen renewable-energy goals

European Parliament says that 35% of energy should be clean by 2030 — but target is not yet legally binding.
>The EU is the world’s third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases after China and the United States, releasing about 10% of global emissions.

News from Science

Citizen scientists comb through data from distant stars, discover five planets

In a first for citizen science, a team of amateur astronomers—comprising thousands of volunteers—has discovered an entire planetary system, BBC News reports. The discovery was announced this week at the 231st meeting of the American Astronomical Society in Washington, D.C.

Fields Medal was never meant for ‘the greatest mathematical genius’

The letters suggest the award was never intended to honor the most important discoveries in the field, but was meant to recognize promising up-and-coming talents. “It’s basically exactly the opposite of how the Fields works today,” says Michael Barany, a historian of mathematics at Dartmouth College, who reported the discovery last week at the Joint Mathematics Meeting in San Diego, California.

Make replication studies ‘a normal and essential part of science,’ Dutch science academy says

Scientists, universities, funding agencies, and journals alike should be doing much more to ensure the reproducibility of scientific research, according to a new report released Monday by the Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences (KNAW). The report also highlights the need to change the incentive structure in publishing and funding. Journals and grant agencies should take into account the rigor of a study, and not only reward innovative methods or novel findings. (In a newspaper interview yesterday, Mackenbach said that between 5% and 10% of research funding should eventually be spent on replication studies.) Journals should also devote more space to publish both replication studies and null results, the group says.

Although in this news, the conclusion is that current mathematic model cannot make a better decision than hummanbeings. But what if they can in the future? Should we give the power to a machine to determine a humanbeing’s fate? This is like a movie scenario to me.

This ‘muscular’ mayfly has arms like airplane wings

The mayfly lives in fast-flowing river currents, grazing on algae that blankets the rocky riverbed. Rather than sheltering itself from the flow,the insect crawls onto exposed rocks to face the torrent head-on. The truth is that the leg has a shape similar to an upside-down airplane wing. Therefore, water folwing over the leg pushes the nymph mayfly toward the groud, rather than lifting it off the rock. It’s an evolutionary trick that just goes to show: If you can’t beat the current, you might as well use it.

Nearly 100 scientists spent 2 months on Google Docs to redefine the p-value. Here’s what they came up with

We always choose 0.05 for p value as the standard to decide if our assumption is statistically ture or not becasue we learn it from our textbook. But why 0.05? when not 0.01 or 0.005 or any other value? This is a hard question, but it is necessary to keep it in mind that there is no solid reasons for choosing 0.05 currently. Researchers are trying to set up a new value, but they are in debat.

You could soon be manufacturing your own drugs—thanks to 3D printing

Amazing! Scientists are designing a 3D machine that can print medicine! In this case, consumers can print their own medicines at home if they got the machine and the right recipes. There will be negative effect like some dangerous drugs might be out of control without a good management, but it should not prevent beneficial uses that could save many lives.

This week in Science

Watching single molecules in motion

Structural techniques such as x-ray crystallography and electron microscopy give insight into how macromolecules function by providing snapshots of different conformation states. But they cannot see the movement of single molecules. In contrast, Single-molecule Forster resonance energy transfer (smFRET) that was first implemented in 1996 make this possible. For example, smFRET has been utilized to provide mechanistic insights of DNA repair, transcritptin, and translation.

Editors’ Choice

How hunting affects brown bear populations

The study of how a brown bear population in Sweden is affected by hunting has been monitored continuously since 1985. The study shows that hunting was the leading cause of death for bears aged more than 3 years, resulting in reduced life expectancy. Hunting also substantially reduces the reproductive value-that is, the number of future offspring that female bears of a given age are expected to have.

> Employment requiring high math skills but low social skills, including many science and engineering jobs, has decreased in the United States as high social skills have become increasingly powerful predictors of employment and wage growth.

Letters

The pitfalls of taking science to the public

Be careful before talking your labwork in public media! >Daily newspapers play a fundamental role in informing the public about research findings and discussions. Scientists, in turn, are increasingly expected to interact with the media. However, this system is devoid of peer review, and it is the editors, not authors, that often have the final say on how a piece is worded.

Have your momos and eat them, too

Suprised by the scenarios that a group of Tibetan Buddhist monks eat meat at a monastery in southern India? Acutally, there is no requirement for individual to be vegetarian, even in monastery. Do not be suprised next time when you see a monk eating his meat. >Although no meat is served in the monastery dining hall, individual monks can choose to eat meat elsewhere, including the monastery’s guesthouse where I and my fellow scientists are staying.

Remote control of nanoscale devices

Scientist are trying to manupelate nanodevices for communication and computation. Recently, DNA nanotechnology has emerged as a powerful tool to build structures precise enough to help us do so. Kopperger et al. show that they are now able to control the motion of a DNA origami device from the outside by applying electric fields.
