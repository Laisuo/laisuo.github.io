24-629 Direct Solar and Thermal Energy Conversion

Posted on 2018-01-31 | In Subject/Course | |

Instructors: Prof. Sheng Shen

Course description

This course introduces principles and technologies for directly converting heat and solar light into electricity using solid-state devices. The first part of the course reviews for understanding quantum mechanics, solid state physics and semiconductor device physics for understanding solid-state energy conversion. The second part discusses the underlying principles of thermoelectric energy conversion, and photovoltaics. Various solar thermal technologies will be reviewed, following by an introduction to the principles of solar thermophotovoltaics and solar thermoelectrics. Spectral control techniques which are critical for solar thermal systems will also be discussed.

Particles

Microscopic Particles (Energy Carriers)

  1. electron $\longrightarrow$ electricity
  2. Photon $\longrightarrow$ solar energy
  3. Phono $\longrightarrow$ thermal energy (heat)
    • Both phonon and electron can carry heat

Classical particles and waves

  1. Particles
    • States: position ($\overrightarrow{F}=m\overrightarrow{a}$), monentum ($P=mV$)
  2. Waves
    • States: wave amplitude, frequency
    • How to tell a wave is a wave? (Answer: diffraction (single slit experiment), or interference (double slit experiment))
    • The solution of wave function $$\overrightarrow{\Phi}=Acos(kx-wt) \overrightarrow{y}$$ where $k=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}$ called wavevector, $\lambda$ called wavelength, $1/\lambda$ called wavenumber.
    • Wave in 3D space $$\overrightarrow{\Phi}=Acos(\overrightarrow{k} \overrightarrow{x}-wt) \overrightarrow{r}$$ where $\overrightarrow{k}=k_x\widehat{x}+k_y\widehat{y}+k_z\widehat{z}$ is wave vector in 3D, $\overrightarrow{r}=x\widehat{x}+y\widehat{y}+z\widehat{z}$
  3. Wave-particle duality
    • Planck-Einstein relations: $$E=\hbar \omega$$ $$\overrightarrow{P}=\hbar \overrightarrow{k}$$ The left side describes particle properties, while the right side wave properties. $\hbar=h/{2 \pi}$
  4. Material waves
    • de Broglie waves $$\lambda=\frac{h}{p}$$ where $h$ is planck constant that is $6.6*10^{-34} Js$, p is momentum.

Quantum mechanics

Schrodinger equation (wave equation)

  1. Governing equation $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+U\psi=i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi$$ where m is mass, U is potential and $U=U(\overrightarrow{r},t)$, $i$ is imaginary unit, and**$\psi=\psi(\overrightarrow{r},t)$ is wave function**.
    • The meaning of $\psi$ itself does not have any physical meaning, but it can be used to calculated all properties of a particle.
  2. Normalization of wave function: if $\psi$ is the solution of schrodinger equation, the $5\psi$, $10\psi$,…, $n\psi$ will also be it’s solution. To obtain a unique solution, we need to normalization it by defining the totally probability to find a particle is 1. $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty \psi^* \cdot \psi d\forall$$ where $d\forall=dxdydz$
  3. Born rule: The simplest form it states that the probability density of finding the particle at a given point is proportional to the square of the magnitude of the particle’s wavefunction at that point. $$P=|\psi|2=\psi*(\overrightarrow{r},t)\cdot \psi(\overrightarrow{r},t)$$
  4. Expectation value: In quantum mechanics, the expectation value is the probabilistic expected value of the result (measurement) of an experiment. It is not the most probable value of a measurement. $$=\int_{-\infty}^\infty \psi^* \Omega \psi$$ where $\Omega$ is called operator. In quantum mechanics, any obserable or measureable quantity has one corresponding operator.
    • Position: $\Omega=\overrightarrow{r}$
    • Momentum: $\Omega=-i\hbar \nabla$
    • Energy: $\Omega=H=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2+U$, where H is called Hamiltonian
  5. Heisonberg Uncertainty Principle: it asserting a fundamental limit to the precision with which certain pairs of physical properties of a particle, known as complementary variables, such as position x and momentum p, can be known. (although we cannot prove it from mathmatic yet, but it has been proved many times in experiment). $$\Delta P_x \cdot \Delta x \geq \hbar/2$$ $$\Delta E \cdot \Delta t \geq \hbar/2$$ These say that position (x) and momentum § or Energy (E) and time (t) cannot be accurately determined simultanously in the quantum mechanics world.
    • $\Delta x=\sqrt{(x-\bar{x})^2}$

Steady-state Schrodinger equation (Time independent Schrodinger equation)

If the potential $U=U(\overrightarrow{r},t)$ is time independent, and $U=U(\overrightarrow{r},t)$=$U=U(\overrightarrow{r})$, we can assume $$\psi(\overrightarrow{r},t)=\psi(\overrightarrow{r}) \cdot Y(t)$$ Substitute it into Schrodinger equation $-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+U\psi=i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi$, we have $$\frac{1}{\psi} (-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+U\psi)=i\hbar \frac{dY}{dt}$$ To make the equation correct, both side should equal to a constant E, which represent energy. $$i\hbar \frac{dY}{dt}=E$$ $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+U\psi=E \psi$$ Finally, we obtain the steady-state Schrodinger equation $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+ (U-E)\psi=0$$
Here are some examples of steady-state Schrodinger equation.

Free particle

The free partile does not have potential, thus $U=0$. Then, Schrodinger equation becomes $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+ E\psi=0$$ The solution of this equation is $$\psi(x)=Ae{-ikx}+Be{ikx}$$ where $k=\sqrt{\frac{2Em}{\hbar^2}}$

Particle in a one-dimensional infinit potential well

Schrodinger equation $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+ (U-E)\psi=0 $$ When $x<0$ or $x>D$, $\psi(x)=0$
When $0<x<D$ $$\psi(x)=Ae^{-ikx} + Be^{ikx} \tag{1}$$ where $k=\sqrt{\frac{2Em}{\hbar^2}}$
To solve the constants A and B, we need two boundary conditions.

Generally, boundary conditions are determined by the continuity of the wave function and their first-order derivations at boundaries.

Here, we have $$\psi(x=0)=0$$ $$\psi(x=D)=0$$ Substitute them into equation (1), we have $$A+B=0 \tag{2}$$ $$Ae^{-ikD} + Be^{ikD}=0 \tag{3}$$
Solving equations (2) and (3), we have $sin(kD)=0$, which means $\sqrt{\frac{2Em}{\hbar^2}} \cdot D = n \cdot \pi$
Therefore $$E_n=\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}(\frac{n \pi}{D})^2 \tag{4}$$
Equation (4) tells us energy is quantized for a particle in a one-dimensional infinit potential well.
Wave function can also be obtained $$\psi(x)=-2iAsin(\frac{n \pi x}{D})$$ The value of A can be calculated with normalization of the wave function and $A=\frac{i}{sqrt{2D}}$
Therefore, wave function is $$\psi(x)=\sqrt{\frac{2}{D}}sin(\frac{n \pi x}{D}) \tag{5}$$
With wave function, all other properties can be calculated using corresponding operator.

Particle in a square nanowire (2D case)

As a square, we can define $L_x=D$ and $L_y=D$. Schrodinger equation is $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} (\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x2}+\frac{\partial2 \psi}{\partial y^2})+ (U-E)\psi=0 \tag{1}$$
The potential within square is 0, that is $U=0$.
Using a mathematic trick, separation of variable, that we define $\psi(x,y)=X(x)Y(y)$, Schrodinger equation can be converted to $$\frac{1}{X} \frac{d^2 X}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{Y} \frac{d^2 Y}{dy^2} + \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}=0 \tag{2}$$
The only way to make equaiton (2) correct is that $$\frac{1}{X} \frac{d^2 X}{dx^2}= - \alpha^2 \tag{3}$$ and $$\frac{1}{Y} \frac{d^2 Y}{dy^2}= - \beta^2 \tag{4}$$ where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are constants
Equation (3) gives solution $$X(x)=Asin(\alpha x)+Bcos(\alpha x) \tag{5}$$
To solve parameters A and B, we can use boundary conditions that $\psi = 0$ at $x=0$ and $x=D$ as we did in the one-dimensional potential well, which gives
$$B=0 \tag{6}$$ $$Asin(\alpha D)=0 \tag{7}$$
Equation (7) gives $\alpha_n=\frac{n \pi}{D}$ ($n=1,2,…$)
Therefore, $$X_n(x)=A_nsin(\frac{n \pi}{D} x) \tag{8}$$
Similarly, we also have $$Y_l(y)=\beta_l sin(\frac{l \pi}{D}y) \tag{9}$$
Substitute equation (8) and (9) into (2), we have energy $$E_{nl}=\frac{(l2+n2) \pi^2 \hbar2}{2mD2} \tag{10}$$
$$\psi_{nl}(x,y)=C_{nl}sin(\frac{n \pi}{D}x)sin(\frac{l \pi}{D}y) \tag{11}$$ where $n,l=1,2,…$ and $C_{nl}= \frac{2}{D}$ based on normalization.

Degeneracy: many quantum states at the same energy level. In the above example, $E_{21}=E_{12}$, but $\psi_{21} \neq \psi_{12}$.

Harmonic oscillator

A simple harmonic oscillator is an oscillator that is neither driven nor damped. It consists of a mass m, which experiences a single force, F, which pulls the mass in the direction of the point x=0 and depends only on the mass’s position x and a constant k.
The energy expression for Harmonic oscillator is $U(x)=\frac{1}{2}kx^2$
Therefore, Schrodinger equation for Harmonic oscillator is $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+ (\frac{1}{2}kx^2-E)\psi=0$$
Applying boundary conditions that $\psi=0$ at $x \rightarrow \infty$ and $x \rightarrow -\infty$, we can solve the energy $$E_n=(n+1/2) \hbar \omega$$
where $n=0, 1, 2, …$
When $n=0$, $E_0=\frac{1}{2}\hbar \omega$ that is called zero point energy, which is the lowest energy for any Harmonic oscillator. It needs to be mentioned that when $T=0 K$, $E=E_0$ rather than 0.

  • The harmonic oscillator model represents quantized electromagnetic field (photons) and atomic vibration in solid (phonos).

Rigid motor

The rigid rotor is a mechanical model that is used to explain rotating systems.

Electron energy levels of a hydrogen atom

The coulombic electrostatic force $$F=\frac{1}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0} \cdot \frac{e2}{r2}=\frac{c_1}{r^2}$$
where $c_1=\frac{e^2}{4 \pi \varepsilon_0}$
This gives the potential of electron $$U®=-\int_{r}^\infty Fdr=-\frac{c_1}{r}$$
Therefore, Schrodinger equation of electron is $$-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi+ (-\frac{c_1}{r}-E)\psi=0$$

Electron spin and Pauli exclusive principle

Electron spin

In quantum mechanics and particle physics, spin is an intrinsic form of angular momentum carried by elementary particles, composite particles (hadrons), and atomic nuclei. It can have two states, spin up $s=\frac{1}{2}$ and spin down $s=-\frac{1}{2}$

Pauli exclusive principle

Each quantum state can be occupied by at most one electron.

Statistical mechanics

Statistical mechanics is a branch of theoretical physics that uses probability theory to study the average behaviour of a mechanical system whose exact state is uncertain.

Ensembles

An ensemble is the collection of all the microscopic states of a system. Here, we are going to introduce three types of ensembles that would be useful to describe the distribution of electron, photon, and phonon.

Microcanonical ensemble

This ensemble is an isolated system with fixed energy (U), volume (V), and number of particle (N).
Suppose there are $\Omega$ quantum states, the probability of each state is $P_1$, $P_2$, …, $P_\Omega$. We have Principle of equal probability for the Microcanonical ensemble that $$P_1=P_2=…=P_i=…=P_\Omega$$
Therefore, $P_i=\frac{1}{\Omega}$
The probability for any property $x$ of the ensemble is $$\langle x \rangle=\sum_\Omega P_ix_i=\frac{\sum_\Omega x_i}{\Omega}$$
Boltzman Principle: How to link microscopic world with macroscopic thermodynamic quantities? $$S=k_B ln\Omega$$ where $S$ is entropy (macroscopic quantity), $\Omega$ is the number of microscopic state (microscopic world), $k_B=1.38\times 10^{-23} J/K$ Boltzman constant.

Canonical ensemble

This ensemble allows energy exchange with surroundings, while Temperature (T), volume (V) and number of particle (N) are fixed.
The probability of quantum state $$P(E_i)=\frac{exp(-\frac{E_i}{k_B T})}{Z}$$ where $Z=\sum_i exp(-\frac{E_i}{k_B T})$ that is called canonical partition function.

Grand canonical ensemble

This ensemble not only allows energy exchange but also particle number exchange with surroundings, while Temperature (T) and volume (V) are fixed.
The probability of quantum state $$P(E_i, N_i)=\frac{exp(\frac{\mu_{N_i}-E_i}{k_B T})}{\Pi}$$ where $\Pi=\sum_{N_i} \sum_{E_i} exp(\frac{\mu_{N_i}-E_i}{k_B T})$ that is called Grand cannonical partition function.

Note: These ensembles are the fundation of electron, photon, phonon distribution.

Classic distributions

Fermi-Dirac distribution (Electron)

The distribution of electron follows Pauli exclusion principle, which means each quantum state can only have at most one electron. Assume there are $\Omega$ energy states, and we are study energy state $i$, which is our system. All other energy states except $i$ are reservor. In such case, this is a Grand canonical ensemble becasue electron can jump from state i to another state.
From Pauli exclusion principle, the number of electron at state $i$ can be 0 or 1. Therefore, the Grand cannonical partition function $$\Pi=\sum_{N_i} \sum_{E_i} exp(\frac{\mu_{N_i}-E_i}{k_B T})=1+exp(\frac{\mu-E_i}{k_B T})$$
The probability of state $i$ with energy E $$P(N_i=0, E_i=0)=\frac{1}{\Pi}=\frac{1}{1+exp(\frac{\mu-E}{k_B T})}$$ $$P(N_i=1, E_i=E)=\frac{exp(\frac{\mu-E}{k_B T})}{\Pi}=\frac{exp(\frac{\mu-E}{k_B T})}{1+exp(\frac{\mu-E}{k_B T})}$$
The particle number at quantum state $i$ $$\langle n \rangle=f(E)=\sum_i P_i N_i=\frac{1}{1+exp(\frac{E- \mu}{k_B T})}$$
Any particle that obey Fermi-Dirac distribution are called “Fermions”.

  • Here, we further introduce a concept “Fermi Level”. “Fermi level” is the term used to describe the top of the collection of electron energy levels at absolute zero temperature. Electrons are fermions and by the Pauli exclusion principle cannot exist in identical energy states. So at absolute zero they pack into the lowest available energy states and build up a “Fermi sea” of electron energy states. The Fermi level is the surface of that sea at absolute zero where no electrons will have enough energy to rise above the surface.

Bose-Einstein distribution (Phonons, Photons)

Compared to electron, phonon and photon (1) does not follow Pauli exclusion princlipe, and (2) particle number is not conserved that means they can be created or vanished. Since the number of particle is not conserved, we can assume there is no particle change between system and reservior. Therefore, canonical ensemble can discribe their distribution.
Both photon and phonon are quantum oscillators. From previous lecture, we now oscillator has quantized energy level $E_n=(n+\frac{1}{2}) \hbar \omega$ where $n=0, 1, 2, …$.
Note, the value $n$ here not only stand for quantum state, but also represents the number of phonon or photon because energy is proportional with the number when frequency is constant ($\omega$).
Therefore, canonical partition function $$Z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty exp(-\frac{(n+\frac{1}{2})\hbar \omega}{k_B T})=\frac{exp(-\frac{\hbar \omega}{2k_B T})}{1-exp(\frac{- \hbar \omega}{k_B T})}$$
Probability $$P=\frac{exp(-\frac{(n+\frac{1}{2})\hbar \omega}{k_B T})}{Z}=exp(-\frac{n\hbar \omega}{k_B T})[1-exp(-\frac{-\hbar \omega}{k_B T})]$$
Average particle number $$\langle n \rangle=f(E)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty nP=\frac{1}{exp(\frac{\hbar \omega}{k_B T})-1}$$
Any particle that obey “Bose-Einstein” distribution are called “Bosons”. Photon and phonon are both Bosons.

Quick summary

The following two conclusions are extremely important.
* Distribution of Fermions like electron: $$\langle n \rangle=f(E)=\sum_i P_i N_i=\frac{1}{1+exp(\frac{E- \mu}{k_B T})}$$
* Distribution of Bosons like photons and phonons: $$\langle n \rangle=f(E)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty nP=\frac{1}{exp(\frac{\hbar \omega}{k_B T})-1}$$
