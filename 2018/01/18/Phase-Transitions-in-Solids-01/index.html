Lecture 1 Introduction to Phase Transformation

Definations

What is phase?
- A phase is a physically distinct, homogeneous portion of a thermodynamic system delineated in space by a bounding surface, an interphase interface; it is distinguished by its state of aggregation (solid, liquid or gas ), symmetry, crystal structure, composition and/ or degree of order. Each phase in a material system generally exhibits a characteristic set of physical, mechanical and chemical properties often characterized by order parameters (\(\eta_1, \eta_2\) etc.) and is, in principle, mechanically separable from the whole.
- A phase is a physically distinct, homogeneous portion of a thermodynamic system delineated in space by a bounding surface, an interphase interface …..in principle, mechanically separable from the whole.
- A phase is ……..distinguished by its state of aggregation (solid, liquid or gas ).

A change of phase occurs when one or more of its order parameters changes from zero to a non-zero value. The subsequent change in order parameter below the transition may occur continuously or discontinuously.

What is a transformation? - The action of changing in form, shape, or appearance; metamorphosis. (from Oxford English Dictionary)

What is phase transformation?
- A PHASE TRANSFORMATION in a material system occurs when one or more of the phases in a system changes its (their) state of aggregation, symmetry, crystal structure, degree of order, composition resulting from a reconfiguration of the constituent particles (atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, etc.) comprising the phase.
- Note: there are two types of phase transformations, continuous and discontinuous phase transformation.

Why, How fast, What to, and How?

Why: thermodynamics.

  • In thermodynamics, we are interested in the opposite question that is why or when is a phase at equilibrium (no phase transformation). The second law of thermodynamics tells us that the equilibrium value of any unconstrained internal parameter is such as to maximize the entropy (S) for the given value of the total energy (U).
  • So there are equivalent ways of stating the what happens when a phase spontaneously changes: we can say that entropy of the universe must increase or that the Gibbs Free Energy (at constant T and P) of the system or phase) must decrease. \[\Delta S_universe>0 or \Delta G_{\beta}<0\]
  • Stability criteria for thermodynamic potentials: (Use the thermodynamic square):
    \[(\frac{\partial ^2 U}{\partial S^2}) \geq 0, (\frac{\partial ^2 U}{\partial V^2}) \geq 0\] \[(\frac{\partial ^2 F}{\partial T^2}) \leq 0, (\frac{\partial ^2 F}{\partial V^2}) \geq 0\] \[(\frac{\partial ^2 H}{\partial S^2}) \geq 0, (\frac{\partial ^2 H}{\partial P^2}) \leq 0\] \[(\frac{\partial ^2 G}{\partial T^2}) \leq 0, (\frac{\partial ^2 G}{\partial P^2}) \leq 0\] Concave functions of intensive variables, like T and P. Convex functions of extensive variables, like S and V.

How fast: Kinetics

  • For the most part the rate of transformation is related to either rates of diffusion or the speed of sound.
    \[X=1-exp[-(kt)^n]\]

To what

  • Structure change, like from BCC to FCC
  • Property difference, like from paraelectric to ferroelectric

How: Mechanism

  • Phase transformation can be divided into (1) disruptic (or reconstructive), (2) Non-disruptic that includes replacive and displacive.
  • Besides, phase transformation can also be divided into two catagories, discontinuous and continuous. They differ in thermodynamics, kinetics, microstructure, and properties.

Important Concepts Need to be learnt for understanding this lecture

Order parameter

An order parameter is a measure of the degree of order across the boundaries in a phase transition system; it normally ranges between zero in one phase (usually above the critical point) and nonzero in the other. From a theoretical perspective, order parameters arise from symmetry breaking. When this happens, one needs to introduce one or more extra variables to describe the state of the system. - An example of an order parameter is the net magnetization in a feromagnetic system undergoing a phase transition.

Space group

In mathematics, physics and chemistry, a space group is the symmetry group of a configuration in space, usually in three dimensions. In three dimensions, there are 219 distinct types, or 230 if chiral copies are considered distinct. Wiki
- 32 crystallographic point groups * 14 Bravais lattices ->230 different space groups, which describes all possible crystal symmeries.

Notation: There are at least ten methods of naming space groups. International symbol is introduced here.
- International symbol or Hermann–Mauguin notation: this notation describes the lattice and some generators for the group. It usually consists of a set of four symbols. The first describes the centering of the Bravais lattice (P, A, B, C, I, R or F). The next three describe the most prominent symmetry operation visible when projected along one of the high symmetry directions of the crystal.
- Bravais lattice: P-Primitive, lattice points on the cell corners only. A, B, C-Base centered. I-Body centered. F-Face centered.
