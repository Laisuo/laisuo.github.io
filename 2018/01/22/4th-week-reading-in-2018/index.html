News from Science

  • Watch this wearable electronic skin control virtual objects: People will have the ability to communicate with virtual world with the help of wearable devices.
  • Oddball scientists, the rise of Chinese research, and other highlights from NSF’s new tome of essential science statistics: The U.S. global share of [science and technology] activities is declining as other nations—especially China—continue to rise.
  • A paper showing how to make a smallpox cousin just got published. Critics wonder why: Smallpox virus, a major human scourage, vanquished in 1980. But now, some scientists published a paper announcing that they successfully synthesize the smallpox virus. Why did they do it? The paper will give terrorists a recipe to construct smallpox virus, which will make humanbeings in danger again. But the authors of the paper do not agree with the opinion. The debat will keep going.
  • How roosters protect themselves from their own deafening crows: A rooster’s crow is so loud that it can deafen you if you stand too close. So how do the birds keep their hearing? Research found out that when a rooster’s beak is fully open, as it is when crowing, a quarter of the ear cannal completely closes and soft tissue covers 50% of the eardrum. Therefore, roosters aren’t capable of hearing their own crows at full strength.
  • Dolphins need to eat up to 25 kilograms of fish every day: Bottlenose dolphins burn as many as 33,000 calories—equivalent to 25 kilograms of fish—every day, BBC News reports.

  • Does cloud seeding really work? An experiment above Idaho suggests humans can turbocharge snowfall: It has been proposed by atmospheric scientific Bernard Vonnegut that particles of silver iodide can cause supercool clouds of water vapor to freeze into snow in the lab in the 1940s. However, it takes 80 years to identify the chain of events from seeding to precipitation on the groud. The difficulties includes (1) Instrucments of decades past couldnot measure water droplet size in clouds, which makes scientists unsure wheter the silver iodide was doing anthing at all. (2) It is impossible to repeat experiment becasue you’ll never have the same atmospheric conditions again. Today, Friedrich and her colleagues applied new instruments and found the evidence that silver iodide do help.

  • Giant centipedes can kill prey 15 times bigger than they are, thanks to this critical toxin: Scientists has identified a nasty toxin in centipede venom that wreaks havoc on the cardiovascular, respiratory, and nervous systerms of other animals-allowing centipedes kill mice 15 times their size in 30 seconds flat. They found that something called Ssm Spooky Toxin shut off the channels that pump potassium in and out of cells—channels that are critical for sending brainwaves, maintaining a regular heartbeat, and controlling a whole host of other bodily functions.

  • U.S. scientists breathe a sigh of relief as government shutdown to end: The U.S. Senate today voted 81–18 to advance legislation funding government activities through 8 February, essentially ending a partial government shutdown that had moved into its third day.

  • New smart windows darken in the sun—and generate electricity at the same time: Think about a window that can change color and collect solar energy at the same time. Isn’t it cool? The idea of this “Smart windows”have been around for decades actually. But it failed to make a broad impact in the building market because of it high cost, inconsisteencies in its ability to block outside light and the demand for external electrical power. Recently, Perovskites attract researchers attention, and the first advance in combining all features came in November 2017 when researchers (chemist Nathan Neale and mechanical engineer Lance Wheeler) report they had created a lead-based perovskit solar window that switched from transparent to opaque when the temperature hit 60°C. The problem is that the efficiency of the solar cells drops after only a few times switching back and forth, probabably becasue the methylamine fails to fully return the perovskit to its starting crystalline arrangement. Recently, Peidong Yang beat them by reporting that his team has created a cesium-based perovskit solar window that turns oaque and produces electricity when heated, but without methylamine.

  • India’s education minister assails evolutionary theory, calls for curricula overhaul: India’s minister for higher education, Satyapal Singh, took aim at the theory of evolution. Calling himself “a responsible man of science,” Singh, a chemist, suggested that Darwin’s theory is “scientifically wrong” and “needs to change” in school and university curricula. (Is this person serious? Evolution theory might not be perfect, but it is the best theory for now. Please propose a better theory before throwing it away.)

  • A solar shield could save us from climate change. But its sudden collapse would doom the planet: To stave off the heat, some scientists have proposed blanketing Earth in a sheet of sunlight-reflecting particles called aerosols. This solar shield could cool the planet and buy us time, but a new study suggests that if politicians turned off the hypothetical cloud, they could plunge the planet into a sudden ecological Armageddon. (The scene of the ending of the world that happens in movie suddenly shows up in my mind after reading this article)

  • These smart contacts can monitor the glucose in tears: Now, a new study shows that smart contacts are getting closer to reality. Lenses with pliable, transparent electronics can measure glucose levels from tears in the eye—and warn wearers if they get too high. The lenses have been tested only in animals. But if they work in people, they could offer diabetics a pain-free way to monitor when and whether their glucose levels go out of whack, something that could send them tumbling into a coma, or worse.

  • ‘Princess Leia’ holograms one step closer to reality: Scientists have tried to create 3D projections, or volumetric displays, for more than a century with varying degrees of success. Now, a team of engineers has come one step closer to the ideal by exploiting the phenomenon of photophoresis, in which small, airborne particles can be manipulated with an intense beam of light.

  • With successful test fire, massive Falcon Heavy rocket is poised to boost space science: SpaceX CEO Elon Musk has said he’ll consider it a win if his enormous new Falcon Heavy rocket even escapes the launch pad. Today, the rocket fired its engines in a test at the Kennedy Space Center in Florida, clearing the way for an inaugural launch in the coming weeks. The business case for Falcon Heavy involves taking commercial satellites cheaply to low-Earth orbit, and sending tourists on slingshots around the moon. Other possible targets for Falcon Heavy include Saturn’s moons Titan and Enceladus and the ice giants Neptune and Uranus. Astronomers are also thinking about what heavy lift can do for them.

  • Physicists are planning to build lasers so powerful they could rip apart empty space: Inside a cramped laboratory in Shanghai, China, physicist Ruxin Li and colleagues are breaking records with the most powerful pulses of light the world has ever seen. This year, Li and colleagues intend to start building a 100-PW laser known as the Station of Extreme Light (SEL). By 2023, it could be flinging pulses into a chamber 20 meters underground, subjecting targets to extremes of temperature and pressure not normally found on Earth, a boon to astrophysicists and materials scientists alike. The laser could also power demonstrations of a new way to accelerate particles for use in medicine and high-energy physics.

- A framework for more ductile magnesium: Development of ductile magnesium alloys is key to their use in reducing the weight of vehicles and other applications.   • European court suggests relaxed gene-editing rules: Judicial opinion says restrictive regulations may not apply to plants and animals bred using CRISPR technique. If gene-edited products are deemed to be subject to the directive, the required checks and controls would make it too expensive for European academics and small companies to develop such products for the market. Bobek’s text says that there is nothing in the directive to stop member states from making their own rules for gene editing, raising the possibility of a patchwork of different rules applying in different member states, which critics say undermines the single market.

  • Women edged out of last-named authorships in top journals: A Prestige Index exposes the gender bias in author lists published in top science journals. Women are significantly under-represented as last authors on high-quality research papers, according to a recent analysis of the journals tracked by the Nature Index.

  • Science has a problem: Researchers and government agencies pay too little attention to pathological gambling. This must change. The question is that which agency should take the lead? Gambling doesn’t attack any particular organ in the body in the way that smoking attacks lungs, and alcohol the liver. Irrespective of possible blurring of responsibilities, each government needs to make a call and assign the problem of pathological gambling to an appropriate agency or ministry. And more scientists must respond to the very real need to assess and understand the implications, in the same way as those before them have done so admirably on the abuse of alcohol, tobacco and drugs.

  • How brittlestars ‘see’ without eyes: The brittlestar Ophiocoma wendtii, a relative of starfish, can scan the sea floor, thanks to light-sensitive cells scattered across its skin, rather than by using eye-like structures. The researchers first confirmed that Ophiocoma could respond to visual cues. “Not only do they move away from light, but they can pick out a dark shade at a distance of about 40 centimetres and move towards it very rapidly”. “Sensing the environment and responding to a stimulus without having to wait for that signal to go all the way to the brain can save a lot of time,” Sigwart says. And the idea could inspire the development of robots and image-recognition technology that don’t rely on a central control system, she adds.

1/27/2018 2:21:06 PM
Impacts of Variations in Manufacturing Parameters on Performance of Lithium-Ion-Batteries. The studied manufacturing parameters include (1) active material mass loading, (2) calendering stress load, (3) carbon black content; the cathode material includes LMO and NCM. Here are some conclusions. * Among the investigated calendering stress load, the highest calendering configurations yielded the highest discharge capacities, and lowest electronic resistances.
* NMC shows characteristic advantage at low C-rates below 2C, while LMO performs superior at higher C-rates. But this critical value can be shifted by changing calendering stress load.
* Another interesting finding is that calendering enhances the reproducibility of performance among cells for given parameter configurations.

Temperature-Dependence of the Solid-Electrolyte Interphase Overpotential: Part I. Two Parallel Mechanisms, One Phase Transition. This paper studied resistance of SEI at temperature range of \(-40^oC\) to \(+80^oC\) for Li, Na, K and Rb-metal electrodes in carbonate electrolytes. A critical temperature \(T_c\) was proposed here that phase transition of SEI (inner dense SEI) happens and switching-off the more efficient transport mechanism and leaving only the general ion conduction mechanism when T is lower than \(T_c\).
* overpotentials for Li electrodes can be negligible at room temperature, but it could be a dominate factor at very low temperature.

Direct Detection of Manganese Ions in Organic Electrolyte by UV-vis Spectroscopy. In this study, a technique is introduced that can detecte the concentration of \(Mn^{2+}\) in electrolyte. 4-(2-pyridylazo) resorcinol (PAR) is utilized as a UV-vis probe. Chelation between PAR and \(Mn^{2+}\) ion induces a characteristic absorption peak where the peak intensity correspondes to \(Mn^{2+}\) ion concentration in the electrolyte.
