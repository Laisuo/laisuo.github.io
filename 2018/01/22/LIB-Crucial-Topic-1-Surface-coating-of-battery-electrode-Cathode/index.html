LIB Crucial Topic 1 - Surface coating of battery electrode (Cathode)

Posted on 2018-01-22 | In Research | |

Motivation

  1. The passivating ability of the SEI layer is assumed to be due to the presence of the densely packed solid Li salt-like components at the top of graphite surface (the deepest region of SEI layer); the Li salts and solvent molecules can approach the outside of the solid Li salt-like components through the loosely packed organic layer, but only Li+ ions can cross this densely packed layer. However, a literature survey reveals that the passivating ability of SEI layers cannot be accounted for only by the solid Li salt-like components. More specifically, SEI layers generated from some SEI-forming agents such as vinylene carbonate (VC) contain dense organic layers that include poly(vinylene carbonate), which exhibit excellent passivating abilities. JES 165 (3) A575-A583 (2018)
    • Idea from this paper: Synthesize poly(vinylene carbonate) using iCVD and coat it on cathode/anode surface.

C based material coating

N-C

  • Eco-friendly nitrogen-containing carbon encapsulated LiMn2O4 cathodes to enhance the electrochemical properties in rechargeable Li-ion batteries
    XPS suggested that the N-C coating greatly reduced the dissolution of Mn and EIS showed that the coating greatly suppressed the charge transfer resistance, even after long-term cycling. The control of Mn dissolution and inner resistance allowed faster Li-ion transport between the two electrodes resulting in improved discharge capacity and cycling stability.

Metal Fluoride

$FeF_3$

  • Surface modification of spinel LiMn2O4 with FeF3 for lithium ion batteries
    The 5 wt.% FeF3-coated LiMn2O4 demonstrates the best cycling performance, with the capacity retentions of 68.2% after 200 cycles at room temperature (25 °C) and 61.5% after 100 cycles at elevated temperature (55 °C), much better than those of the pristine materials, 49.8 and 40.2%. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) confirms that FeF3 modification layer improves the structure stability of LiMn2O4. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) data illustrate that FeF3 coating can suppress the fast growth of undesirable solid electrolyte interfacial (SEI) film. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) tests show that the existence of FeF3 helps to enhance the thermal stability of LiMn2O4 cathode.

Background of Surface film formation

  1. Solid Permeable Interface (SPI) on a High-Voltage Positive Electrode of Lithium-Ion Batteries: The result shows that the surface film on graphite is impermeable to electrolyte molecules and highly passivating. In contrast, the surface film on LNMO is highly permeable to molecules and thus poorly passivating.
    1. Previous SEI model: Peled and coworkers proposed a model that describes the SEI as a mosaic-type distribution of organic components including lithium alkyl carbonates (ROCO2Li) and solid Li salt-like components such as Li2O, Li2CO3, and LiF. This model describes that the ill-defined organic components are loosely packed at the outer region of SEI layer, while the solid Li salt-like components are densely packed at its deeper region. The Li salts and solvent molecules can diffuse into the pores of the loosely packed outer layer, but only Li+ ions can cross the compact deeper layer through some Li+ lattice sites of the solid Li salt-like components (Li2O or Li2CO3) by a knock-off mechanism.
    2. Debates: However, a literature survey reveals that the passivating ability of SEI layers cannot be accounted for only by the solid Li salt-like components. More specifically, SEI layers generated from some SEI-forming agents such as vinylene carbonate (VC) contain dense organic layers that include poly(vinylene carbonate), which exhibit excellent passivating abilities. This illustrates that Li salts and solvent molecules cannot penetrate into the deeper regions of VC-derived SEI layers, resulting in sluggish electron tunneling. Currently, clear conclusions on the nature of the passivating ability of the SEI layers cannot be drawn, but it seems very likely that some dense layers of certain thicknesses, which allow Li+ ion transport but block solvent molecules, are responsible for the passivating role of the SEI layer.
