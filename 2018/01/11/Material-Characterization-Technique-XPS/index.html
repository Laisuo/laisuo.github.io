Material Characterization Technique_XPS

Posted on 2018-01-11 | In Experiment , Research | |

Principle

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is based on the photoelectric effect that proposed by Einstein in 1905. When X-rays are projected to the surface of a sample, atomes in the sample are ionized and the kinetic energies of the emitted photoelectrons are measured. In this way, the binding energy of the electron can be calculated.[1] \[E_{binding}=E_{photon}-(E_{kinetic}+\theta)\]
where \(\theta\) is work function of the studied element.
Due to the small mean free path of the emitted electrons in solides (~ 10 nm), XPS is only surface sensitive. But combining with ion sputting, XPS can give depth profiles of a sample.

Limitations

Here are some limitations of XPS when it is utilized to study SEI in LIBs.[2]
(1) Damage sample: caused by high energetic photons
(2) Degradation of SEI components and may alter their nature
(3) Quantification becomes complicated when the substrate and the surface have the same elements
(4) Shift to low energy for insulting samples (charging)
(5) In ion sputtering, the etching rate is not the same for hard and soft components
(6) Etching may also lead to modification of the surface and reactions of the active species to form undersirable products

Shift of Binding Energy

The peak shift in XPS is most of time related to a change of oxidation state of the element your are looking at. Higer binding energies mean higher oxidation states. A good starting point for a literature research for the peak shifts of your material is the XPS database of NIST. Ionizing Radiation

X-rays

  • Characteristic X-rays: Characteristic x-rays are generated when electrons are displaced and fall from higher energy to lower energy electron shells.
  • Bremsstrahlung X-rays: Bremsstrahlung X-rays are created when electrons are slowed down in the field of a nucleus.

Radiation Measurement

Biological effects of ionizing radiation

  • Mechanism of damage: (1) produce free radicals; (2) break chemical bonds; (3) produce new chemical bonds and cross-linkage between macromolecules; (4) damage molecules that regulate vital cell processes, like DNA, RNA, and proteins.
  • Tissue sensitivity: Tissues with cells dividing more frequently are more likely to be affected. Cells that are the least specialized are the most radiosensitive.
  • Occupational radiation exposure limits: (1) Whole body = 5000 mrem/year; (2) Extremities = 50000 mrem/year; (3) Eye = 15000 mrem/year; (4) Fetus = 500 mrem/gestation period.

Radiation Protection

  • Time: decreasing the time spent near a radiation source decreases radiation exposure
  • Distance: increasing the distance from a radiation source decreases radiation exposure
  • Shielding: increasing the shielding of a radiation source decreases radiation exposure. Generally, leaded glass is used to provide shielding at viewing and sample ports.

Book Reading: auger- and x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy in materials science

Preface

  • The aim of this book is mainly to help the practical analyst in his daily work on a rather basic level. Furthermore, it should serve as a guide for students working for their master’s or Ph.D. thesis in materials science by teaching them about capabilities and limitations of applied surface analysis using AES and XPS in their special ﬁeld.

Chapter 1: Introduction and Outline

  • By the middle of the last century, it became obvious that the chemical composition of surfaces and interfaces in atomic dimensions determines many properties of materials. For example, corrosion and oxidation, intergranular brittle fracture, wear and friction, and electronic properties strongly depend on surface and interfacial microchemistry. Auger electron spectroscopy (AES) and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) have outstanding features when compared with other surface and interface analysis techniques. Historically, the foundations of XPS were laid much earlier than that of AES.
  • After 1985, XPS has become far more popular as a tool for materials research, as seen in Fig. 1.1. However, AES still has its appropriate applications, particularly when high spatial resolution is of advantage, as in surface and grain boundary segregation studies and in topics related to depth proﬁling, such as diffusion and interfacial reactions.
  • The spatial resolution of XPS is not very high, the state-of-art is 3-15 μm. In contrast, the AES applies highly focused electron beam that allows spatially resoved investigations similar to SEM, and the state-of-art is 5 nm.
  • A good vedio to illustrate Auger Effect

Chapter 2: Instrumentation

Vacuum System

  1. There are two basic conditions for the need of vacuum in electron spectroscopy: (1) To avoid electron scattering on gas molecules in the path from sample to analyzer; (2) To avoid attenuation and distortion of the spectra by surface contamination
    • Two meet (1), a vacuum of around \(10^{-2}\) Pa (~ \(10^{-4}\) Torr) is need, which gives around \(2m\) of mean free path. \(L_{coll}=\frac{kT}{1.414p \sigma_{coll}}\), for air at 20°C \(L_{coll}(cm)=0.01/p (Torr)\)
    • To meet (2), it requires that adsorption from the residual gas atmosphere does not generate an intolerable amount of contamination on the surface. The time to build up a monolayer (about \(10^{15}\) \(atoms/cm^2\)) is given by \(t_m=\frac{4}{Nvd_m^2}\), where N is the density of the gas (moleculers/\(cm^3\)), v the molecular velocity, and \(d_m\) the molecular diameter. For highly reactive gas with sticking coefficient=1, the monolayer formation time is about \(t_m(s)=1.7 \times 10^{-6}/p (Torr)\). In the pressure range of \(10^{-6}\) Torr, one monolayr is built up in about 1 second, which is intolerable in surface analysis becasue a contamination monolayer reduces the signal of the underlying surface, depending on its energy, by typically 10-50%. Therefore, generally pressures lower than \(5 \times 10^{-8} Pa\) (\(3 \times 10^{-10} Torr\)) is needed.

X-ray Source

The most common X-ray sources used in XPS are equipped with Mg or Al anodes. The characteristic Mg \(K_{\alpha}\) radiation at 1253.6 eV and the Al \(K_{\alpha}\) radiation at 1486.6 eV possess sufﬁciently high energies for core level excitation as well as a sufﬁciently low line width (below 1 eV) to yield XPS spectra with fairly good resolution.

Electron Gun

For application in AES surface analysis, the two most important electron gun characteristics are: (1) High beam current for high sensitivity (limited by spatial resolution and sample damage); (2) Low beam diameter for high spatial resolution (limited by beam current).

Ion Gun

The ion gun in AES and XPS is not the primary excitation source. However, it is necessary for sample cleaning and above all for sputter depth proﬁling.

Electron Energy Analyzer

The most important part of an electron spectrometer is the electron energy analyzer.

Chapter 3: Qualitative Analysis (Principle and Spectral Interpretation)

Intro: Notation of Atomic Electron Levels

The notation of the electrons involved is standardized but different in XPS and AES. Whereas the spectroscopic notation is used in XPS, the X-ray notation is used in AES. A photoelectron can be attributed to one electron level ($nl_j), for example, \(3p_{3/2}\). In contrast, an Auger transition involves three electron levels and is therefore notiﬁed as, for example, by \(KL_{2,3}L_{2,3}\), where the subscripts are often omitted, and by \(KVV\) if the valence band is involved.

Qualitative XPS

\[E_b=hv-E_{kinetic}-\Phi_A\] where\(|Phi_A\) is the work function of the analyzer! 1. Inelastic scattering causes the icreased but smoothly varying background on the high binding energy (= low kinetic energy) side of the main elemental peak. 2. Whereas all s peaks (l=0) are singlets, all other levels (p, d, f) are doublets with slightly different energies. Because the unpaired electron left after photoemission has either a parallel or an antiparallel orientation to the orbital momentum (spin-orbit coupling), there is an energy difference. - The energy separation of the two adjacent peaks of the same orbit (same n, l) increase with atomic number and decreses for the same n with higher l values. - The relativ intensities of the doublet peaks are given by the ratio of their degeneracy (2j+1), as compiled in Table 3.2.

  1. Chemical Shift of Photoelectron Peak Energy: Ignoring final-state effects, the chemical shift can be explained by the effective charge potential change on an atom. For example, when an atom is bounded to anther one with higher electronegativity, a charge transfer to the latter occurs and the effective charge of the former becomes positives, thus increasing the binding energy. Vice versa, the binding energy of the atom with higher electronegativity is decreased.
  2. Auger Parameter: A convenient means to avoid the effect of charging on the identiﬁcation of a chemical bond.
    • Wagner Plot: This plot is important although I do not fully understand it now.
  3. Valence Band Spectra: Valence band spectra represent a convoluted picture of the electron density of states, given by the band structure of solids. Therefore, they can be used to distinguish between different structures of materials. For example, TiO2 shows different valence band spectra in its rutile and anatase structures. Valence band spectroscopy has a yet to be exploited potential for detailed surface chemistry studies.
  4. Satellite Peaks: there are three sources that might introduce satellite peaks
    • Satellite peaks without monochromator
    • Plasmon loss peaks
    • Shake-up, shake-off, and multiplet splitting
  5. XPS Line Shapes: Gaussian-Lorentzian Line Shape, Doniach–Sunjic Line Shape
    • Line Fitting and Peak Width: Correct peak shapes are essential for quantitative evaluation of overlapping spectra
  6. Emission Angle Effects:
    • Amorphous: Crystalline effects can be ignored for amorphous and also for polycrystalline samples, if the crystallite size is much smaller than the analyzed area. A quick qualitative check of surface layer species is possible by comparison of the relative intensities at two different emission angles. (Check the book for details)
    • Single Crystals: can be used to study the structure of the ﬁrst few atomic layers

Qualitative AES

  1. Principle of AES Analysis: \[E_{WXY}=E_W-E_X-E_Y-\Phi_A\], The equation neglects the ionization and relaxation effects which accompany any Auger transition. In principle, \(E_W\) and \(E_X\) are the binding energies for the singly ionized atom, and \(E_Y\) is that of the doubly ionized state, because ﬁnally two electrons have left the atom.
    • Due to the uncertainty principle, \(E_{WXY}\) is of the order of a few eV.
  2. Auger Spectra and Elemental Identiﬁcation: The Auger process is dominant for kinetic energies below 2,000 eV with more than 90% and decreases strongly for higher energies where the ﬂuorescence yield is becoming larger. Therefore, the most prominent Auger peaks are the KLL transitions for elements with atomic number from Z=3 to 14, the LMM transitions for elements Z= 14–40, and MNN transitions for the heavier elements.
  3. Recognition and Inﬂuence of Chemical Bonding: Chemical effects in AES are frequently observed not only by peak energy shift but also by line shape changes. Owing to the dominating inﬂuence of screening and relaxation effects of the ﬁnal two hole state in AES, chemical shifts of Auger energies are often relatively high as compared to XPS.
    • Because of the complexity of Auger spectra, peak decomposition is less frequently applied than principal component analysis (or linear least square ﬁtting of standard spectra) which can be very powerful in quantitative analysis of different bonding states
  4. Electron Backscattering, Channeling, and Diffraction
    • Backscattering: Electron backscattering is responsible for the shape of the background observed in survey spectra and for the excitation of Auger electrons in addition to that of the incoming primary electrons.
    • Channeling and Diffraction: Electrons in a channeling direction penetrate deeper into the crystal and cause less surface excitations than those having nonchanneling directions. The contrast frequently observed between different grains on a clean polycrystalline metallic sample surface in a secondary electron image can be attributed to the channeling effect of primary electrons.
