Posted on 2018-01-11 | In Reading

Principle

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is based on the photoelectric effect that proposed by Einstein in 1905. When X-rays are projected to the surface of a sample, atomes in the sample are ionized and the kinetic energies of the emitted photoelectrons are measured. In this way, the binding energy of the electron can be calculated.[1]
$$E_{binding}=E_{photon}-(E_{kinetic}+\theta)$$
where \(\theta\) is work function of the studied element.
Due to the small mean free path of the emitted electrons in solides (~ 10 nm), XPS is only surface sensitive. But combining with ion sputting, XPS can give depth profiles of a sample.

Limitations

Here are some limitations of XPS when it is utilized to study SEI in LIBs.[2]
(1) Damage sample: caused by high energetic photons
(2) Degradation of SEI components and may alter their nature
(3) Quantification becomes complicated when the substrate and the surface have the same elements
(4) Shift to low energy for insulting samples (charging)
(5) In ion sputtering, the etching rate is not the same for hard and soft components
(6) Etching may also lead to modification of the surface and reactions of the active species to form undersirable products

Shift of Binding Energy

The peak shift in XPS is most of time related to a change of oxidation state of the element your are looking at. Higer binding energies mean higher oxidation states. A good starting point for a literature research for the peak shifts of your material is the XPS database of NIST.
A good place to start to refresh one’s memory about the basic principles of XPS is the wikipedia article
