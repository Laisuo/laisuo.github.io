LIB Crucial Topic 2 - Ni-rich cathode

Posted on 2018-03-19 | In Research | |

Reviews

Manthiram: A perspective on nickel-rich layered oxide cathodes for lithium-ion batteries (2017)

Background Knowledge

Derived from LiNiO2, many LiNixMyO2 cathode materials have been developed to balance the performance of capacity, cyclability, safety, and cost. Where M stands for metal elements, like Fe, Mg, Al, Mn, Co, etc. Here are some good examples.
1. \(LiNi_{1-x}Co_xO_2\): The solid solution with cobalt in \(LiNi_{1-x}Co_xO_2\) within the entire compositional range of x from (0≤x≤1) is readily formed because of the properties similarity of Ni and Co. Importantly, compared to the pure LiNiO2 phase, cobalt substitution is beneﬁcial for reducing the cation disorder and mitigating multiple phase transitions during charge/discharge cycles. As a consequence, the Co-substituted \(LiNi_{1-x}Co_xO_2\) is able to deliver much improved electrochemical properties than the Ni-pure \(LiNiO_2\).

  1. \(LiNi_{1-x}Mn_xO_2\): In contrast to \(LiNi_{1-x}Co_xO_2\), manganese-substituted \(LiNi_{1-x}Mn_xO_2\) solid solutions can only be formed when x is lower than 0.5. Increasing the amount of Mn limits the Li+ insertion, thus the subsitution of Mn is less attractive than Co in term of capacity. But Mn has two advantages, low cost and safety, which makes some groups are still try to optimize the combination of Mn and Ni in the cathode material.
  2. \(LiNi_{1-x-y}Co_xMn_yO_2\): Generally, the \(Ni^{2+/3+}\) and/or \(Ni^{3+/4+}\) redox couples provide the majority of the reversible capacity; Co reinforces the layered ordering with improved rate capability and additional capacity from the \(Co^{3+/4+}\) redox reaction; Mn, on the other hand, remains electrochemically inert in the tetravalent state and stabilizes the local structure, especially at the high delithiated state.
  3. \(LiNi_{1-x}M_xO_2\): M stands for other metal elements, like Fe, Mg, Al, Mn, Co, etc.

Capacity degradation

Ni-rich layered oxides generally show good reversiblity for Li insertion/extraction with little volume change (< 10%). However, degradation at the particle surface causes rapid capacity fading and hurdles their widespread commercialization as cathode materials for LIBs.
1. Surface electrochemical degradation. * Material processing and storage: A native film of \(LiCO_3\) also (\(LiOH\)) is usually present on the particle surface, introduced during materials manufacturing, processing, and storage. The effect of storage time in CO2 on the electrochemical performance of Ni-rich cathodes has been studied [104]. When the cathode material contacts with acidic chemical nature of electrolytes with fluorinated anions, this native film is corroded away instantly and largely replaced by compounds such as LiF, \(Li_xPF_y\), and \(Li_xPO_yF_z\). (The Ni:Co:Mn compositional ratio is a crucial factor in determining the storage stability becasue this ratio essentially determines the \(Ni^{3+}\) content, which is easily reduced to \(Ni^{2+}\) and degrades the air-storage capability.) * Upon cell operation: the surface chemistry on \(LiNi_{1-x}M_xO_2\) becomes more pronounced with a decomposition of existing interphasial species at high voltage, further electrolyte oxidation, and parasitic reactions between the electrolyte components and delithiated cathode particles. (1) thicking of cathode interphases; (2) the deposition of the transition metal dissolution products on the anode.
2. Bulk mechanical degradation: The capacity fade of Ni-rich layered oxides has been primarily ascribed to the deerioration of the electrode-electrolyte interface and irreversible surface phase transition during cell operation. Recent studies, however, have revealed that the mechanical fracture of particles, both secondary and primary, have a major impact on their long-term cyclability as well.

Strategies to improve cell performance

  1. Surface coatings: To date, numerous coating agents have been applied to protect the Ni-rich oxide cathodes surface, including oxides, phosphates, fluorides, carbon, artifical SEIs, and conducting polymers. The mechanism of surface protection has not been fully understood. Here are some proposals from current studies.
    • First, they can protect the surface of Ni-rich oxides
    • Second, the coating agents suppress the active mass dissolution
    • Third, the coating layer mitigates the parasitic reactions of the delithiated Ni-rich oxide surface
    • Last but not the least, it suppresses surface irreversible structural rearrangement.

It is worth noting that, in most cases where surface coating agents are applied, isolated islands of the coated materials are formed rather than a uniform and full coverage. This compromises the protection effect and leads to insufficient long-term cycling stability, especially at elevated temperatures. On the other hand, if the coating is thick enough to cover the entire active material, lithium transport through the electrolyte-electrode interface might be affected, and a much lower accessible reversible capacity is often obtained.

Material Storage leads degradation

  1. Influence of Atmosphere on Electrochemical Performance of LiNi0.8Co0.1Mn0.1O2 Electrodes for Li-Ion Batteries: This work studies Ni-rich positive material and the influence upon exposure to ambient air on surface chemical composition and electrochemical performance.
    • TEM confirms the existence of amorphous surface layer after contacting with atmosphere and the thickness is about 3-4 nm.
    • The charge−discharge curves and electrochemical impedance spectra indicate that exposure to air lead to increased impedance and polarization, which seriously affects LiNi0.8Co0.1Mn0.1O2 cycling properties.
  2. Investigation and improvement on the storage property of LiNi0.8Co0.2O2 as a cathode material for lithium-ion batteries: LiNi0.8Co0.2O2 cathode material showed a performance loss after storage in air. Two thin layers were found on the surface. The ﬁrst layer in contact with the bulk material contains NiO-like species, and the top layer consists of adsorbed hydroxyl, bicarbonate, carbonate, and crystalline Li2CO3. These two layers are both electrochemically inactive and poor conductors for Li+ ions, which are believed to be responsible for the storage loss.
    • For storage loss prevention, a doping approach to reduce nickel content and a storage approach to isolate the material from H2O and CO2 were found to be effective to improve the storage property of LiNiO2-based materials.
    • For storage loss recovery, a heat-treatment process at 725C was demonstrated to be a feasible approach for full recovery of the performance.

  3. Studies on Storage Characteristics of LiNi0.4Co0.2Mn0.4O2 as Cathode Materials in Lithium-Ion Batteries: It was found that the surface reaction between samples and CO2/H2O was so negligible that it could not affect the electrochemical performance of the material. But if storing the material in Ar-filled glove box, the oxygen might release from the surface, and, consequently, Mn and Co ions could decrease their oxidation state, take part in the electrode reaction, and then dissolve into electrolyte during the cycling process. Thus cyclic performance of the samples stored in Ar is poorer than that forsamples stored in other conditions.

  4. Li2CO3 in LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 cathodes and its effects on capacity and power: Lithium carbonate is commonly found on the surfaces of lithiated cathode active materials that have been exposed to air. Long-term exposure of LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 electrodes produced a dense Li2CO3 coating, approximately 10 nm in thickness that severely reduced both the accessible capacity and the rate at which the electrodes could be cycled.

  1. Washing effects on electrochemical performance and storage characteristics of LiNi0.8Co0.1Mn0.1O2 as cathode material for lithium-ion batteries

Coating

Polymer coatings

  1. Surface modification of LiNi0.8Co0.1Mn0.1O2 with conducting polypyrrole: polypyrrole (PPy) coated NCM811 shows better cycling performance and rate performance.

  2. PEDOT modified LiNi1/3Co1/3Mn1/3O2 with enhanced electrochemical performance for lithium ion batteries: Solution processing. To explore the optimum coating conditions, the coating amount was changed from 1 wt% to 4 wt% and the annealing temperature varied from 200 oC to 400 oC.

Inorganic coatings

  1. Enhanced storage property of LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 coated with LiCoO2: After LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 and LiCoO2-coated LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 were stored at the same conditions, the latter showed less changes than the former in the aspects of weight, nickel oxidation state, moisture and carbon contents and electrochemical performance. This is due to the LiCoO2 coating layer suppresses effectively the reactions between LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 and atmosphere, and LiCoO2 has good chemical stability.
    • Our experimental data indicate that storing LiNi0.8Co0.15Al0.05O2 in air with lower relative humidity can improve the storage property of the material within a narrow range. The more effective approach is to coat the material with an electrochemically active layer which is resistant to H2O and CO2 in air.
