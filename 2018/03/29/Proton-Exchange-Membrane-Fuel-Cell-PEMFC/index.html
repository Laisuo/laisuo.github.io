Proton Exchange Membrane Fuel Cell (PEMFC)

Posted on 2018-03-29 | In Research | |

Basics

Components

The following figure shows the components of a proton exchange membrance fuel cell (PEMFC) [1].

  • Bipolar plates: introduce gaseous reactants to the active heart of the cell, harvest the generated electric current, transport the by-produced water and permit controlling the cell temperature.
  • Gas diffusion layer (GDL): ensure effectively reactants diffusion, conduct electrons, and assist in proper water management.
  • Catalyst layer (CL)： help the electrochemical reactions
  • Proton exchange membrane (PEM): transports proton while prohibits electron conduction.
  • Microporous layer (MPL): aviod flooding. MPL is added in the GDL to build a dual-layer GDL.

General. the PEM, CLs, and GDLs comprise an assembly that is usually called the membrane electrode assemble (MEA). The PEM is the core component of PEMFCs. In 1970s, a sulfonated poly-tetrafluoro-ethylene based cation-exchange membrane was first developed by DuPont, named as Nafion, which not only exhibited a two-fold increase in proton conductivity but also extended the lifetime by four orders of magnitued. Since then, this Nafion film soon became a standard for PEMFCs and remains so till today.

Reactions in PEMFCs

Hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR)

The overall half-cell reaction at the anode is hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR): \[H_2 \rightleftharpoons 2H^+ + 2e^- \tag{1}\] The above equation occurs successive steps. Here, we use ‘M’ to represent an active Pt atom site, ‘\(e^-\)’ to represent an electron. These steps are
Tafel step: \[H_2+2M \rightleftharpoons H_{ad} + H_{ad} \tag{2}\] Heyrovsky step: \[H_2 \rightleftharpoons H^+ + H_{ad} + e^- \tag{3}\] Volmer step: \[H_{ad} \rightleftharpoons H^+ + e^- \tag{4}\] It is worth noting that the Tafel step and the Heyrovsky step are in parallel and either ore or both of them may occur. However, the Volmer step must always occur for the successfully overal reaction.

Oxygen reduction reaction (ORR)

The overall reaction: \[O_2 + 4H^+ + 4e^- \rightleftharpoons 2H_2O \tag{5}\] The real reaction is composed by several series or direct steps, as shown below.

The corresponding exchange current density of ORR can be six-order smaller than that of HOR. As a result, the ORR contribute the major part of activation loss in PEMFCs.

Side reactions

  1. Pt dissolution
    Under certain conditions, such as high potential (>0.9 V vs. RHE), the Pt particles can be oxidized to \(Pt^{2+}\): \[Pt+H_2O \rightleftharpoons PtO + 2H^+ + 2e^- \tag{6}\] \[PtO + 2H^+ \rightleftharpoons H_2O + Pt^{2+} \tag{7}\] Following the dissolution, migration and agglomeration of Pt ions may occur, resulting in a loss in the electrochemically active surface area (ECSA).

  2. Carbon corrosion \[C + 2H_2O \rightleftharpoons CO_2 + 4H^+ + 4e^- \tag{8}\] The equilibrium potential of the above reaction is 0.207 V (vs. RHE), indicating the carbon corrosion reaction is thermodynamically possible during PEMFC normal operating conditions. However, kinetically, the carbon corrosion rates at typical operating conditions are very slow. In some extrem condition, like start/stop cycles or open-circuit duration, the high voltage in the cathode will accelerate the rate significally.

Thermodynamics and kinetics in PEMFCs

Thermodynamics

The reversible (theoretically maximum possible) cell potential \(E_r (V)\), can be calculated as \[E_r=- \frac{\Delta g}{nF}\] where \(\Delta g\) (J mol-1) is the change of Gibbs free energy of the overall reaction per mole of hydrogen, n (=2) the number of moles of electrons transferred per mole of hydrogen, and F (=96,487 C mol-1) the Faraday’s constant.
Since the change of Gibbs free energy represents the maximum useful work avaliable from the reaction, the thermal (theoretically maximum possible) efficiency, \(\eta_{th}\), can be defined as
\[\eta_{th}=\frac{\Delta g}{\Delta h}=1-\frac{T \Delta s}{\Delta h}\] where \(\Delta h\) is the change of enthalpy of the overall reaction per mole of hydrogen, T the temperature (K), and \(\Delta s\) the change of entropy of the overall reaction per mole of hydrogen. By using the above eqution, the thermal efficiency can be calculated to be 83% at 25 °C with product water in liquid state. Practical fuel cells can have efficiency close to this theoretical maximum efficiency, while practical heat engines are very difficult to achieve even about 50% of the theoretical maximum (or Carnot) efficiency.

Kinetics

Even though the OCV predicted from thermodynamics is 1.229 V (298 K), the actual voltage is much less than this value. Generally, the overpotential can be divided into four categories. (1) Voltage loss due to the fuel crossover and internal currents, \(\eta_{cro}\). (2) Activation loss at the three-phase boundary due to the sluggishness of the electrochemical reactions, \(\eta_{act}\). (3) Ohmic loss due to the transport of electrons and protons, \(\eta_{ohm}\). (4) Concentration loss due to the species transport, \(\eta_{con}\). The following figure illustrates the overpotential components in an operating PEMFC.[2]

  • The fuel crossover and internal currents are the waste of fuel passing and electron conduction through the electrolyte, respectively. Ideally, the electrolyte should only transport protons, however very small amount of fuel diffusion and electron flow will always be possible.
  • The activation loss is caused by the slowness of the reactions taking place on the surface of the electrodes. A proportion of the voltage generated is lost in driving the electrochemical reaction that transfers the electrons and protons to or from the electrode. In the above figure, it is represented by the initial sharp drop of the cell voltage.
  • The ohimic loss is the straightforward resistance to the transport of electrons and protons through the materials of the electrodes, membranes and the various interconnections.
  • The concentration or mass transport loss results from the change in concentration of the reactants at the surface of the electrodes as they are consumed along the flow channel from the inlet to the outlet.

Species and processes in PEMFCs

As shown below, multiple processes happen simultaneously duirng PEMFC operation.
- The transport of the gas reactants through gaseous pores from gas channel, GDL to the catalyst layer.
- The conduction of electrons through electronic path from current collectors, GDL to the three-phase boundary in the CL.
- The conduction of protons through protonic path from the membrane to the cathodic catalyst layer.
- The condensation, evaporation and transport of liquid water and vapor.
- The conduction of heat primarily through the solid matrix away from the reaction site in the CL.

Liquid water transport

Although water is by-product of a PEMFC, its transport is the most complex process in PEMFCs becasue of following reasons. (1) The multi-states of water in PEMFCs. (2) The different dominating mechanisms of water transport in different components of PEMFCs. (3) The phase change reactions between different states of water. Jiao and Li wrote an excellent review on this topic in 2011.[2]

Water management

Origin and importance of water management

The major cause of the water management issue is the proton conductor - the membrane. Currently, the mostly widely used membrance is the perfluorosulfonic acid (PFSA) polymer membrane, among which Nafion membrane is the most famous. The PFSA polymer membranes have acceptable proton conductivity only when they are well hydrated. In order to hydrate the membrane, humidified reactant gases are often supplied. However, due to the water production from the electrochemical reactions, as well as the low operating temperature of PEMFC (~ 80 °C) that leads to the almost unavoidable water condensation, liquid water may be present and flood the electrode pore regions, giving rise to the so-called water flooding phenomena. The water flooding is most severe in cathode CL due to the fact that water is produced in this layer and the electro-osmotic drag (EOD) also causes water migrating from anode CL to cathode CL.
Therefore, proper membrane hydration without causing electrode ﬂooding by water, commonly referred to as water management, remains one of the major technical challenges of PEMFC, and maintaining the dynamic balance of water in the cell during operation is essential for better water management and stable performance.

Strategy

  1. The simplest way to avoid the water ﬂooding is to operate PEMFC on non-humidiﬁed inlet gases with acceptable performance degradation. This requires fuel cells operate below 60°C, which limits electrochemical reaction kinetics and membrane conductivity.
  2. External humidiﬁcation using liquified gases or external devices to inject water vapor into membrane.
  3. The excessive water needs to be removed or balanced to avoid the water flooding problem and to better hydrate the membrane. Generally, water is removed by pumping air into cathode flow fields. The GDL and CL properties such as thickness, porosity, permeability, wettability, catalyst loading (for CL) and ionomer fraction (for CL) have strong impacts on water removal. One common way is to add polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) to these layers to increase the hydrophobic level to expel water, and inserting a hydrophobic micro porous layer (MPL) between GDL and CL in cathode was also found to be an effective way to push water from cathode CL into membrane [31-34, 41-45].[2]

State of water

The following figure and table summarizes the state of water at different region in PEMFC.

In membrane

The most suitable classification of the water in ionomer is non-frozen water and forzen water. The non-frozen water can be further classified into non-freezable, freezable and free water, based on how tight they are bond to the sulphuric acids (\(H^+SO_3^-\)). The water absorption level of ionomer is often represented as the number of water molecules per \(SO_3^-\) referred to as the water content (\(\lambda\)). (\(\lambda\)=20 in a well hydrated Nafion membrane). An empirical correlation to calculate the maximum allowed amount of non-frozen water content has been given:

\[\lambda_{sat} \begin{cases} =4.834, & \text{T<223.15 K} \\ =[-1.304+0.01479T-3.594 \times 10^{-5} T^2]^{-1}, & \text{if 223.15 K $\leq$ T<$T_N$ } \\ > \lambda_{nf}, & \text{if T $\geq$ $T_N$} \\ \end{cases}\] where \(\lambda_{sat}\) and \(\lambda_{nf}\) are the saturation and non-frozen water content; and T and \(T_N\) are the local temperature and the normal freezing temperature of water (273.15 K). It has been found that there is a certain amount of water (about \(\lambda\)=4.8) that does not freeze.

The proton conductivity of Nafion ionomer can be calculated using non-frozen water content (rather than the total water content).
\[\kappa_{ion}=(0.5130\lambda_{nf}-0.326) exp[1268(\frac{1}{303.15}-\frac{1}{T})]\] where \(\kappa_{ion}\) is the proton conductivity and T (K) the temperature. It should be noticed that the equation is originally correlated based on experimental measurements between 30°C and 80 °C.

In gas diffusion layer

The GDL is usually carbon paper or carbon cloth, and the porosity is around or higher than 0.5. Water can exist in the pore regions of GDL in the forms of vapor, liquid and ice. The transition amoung them depends on temperature and pressure. The saturation pressure of water and temperature can be calculated as follow. \[log_{10}(P_{sat}/101325)=-2.1794+0.02953(T-273.15)-9.1837\times 10^{-5}(T-273.15)^2+1.4454 \times 10^{-7} (T-273.15)^3\]

In catalyst layer

The most complex scenario occurs in CL. The carbon and platinum particles have to be present as the catalyst and for electron transport, and the ionomer has to be present for proton transport. The volume fraction of the ionomer in CL ranges from about 0.2 to 0.4, and the porosity of the CL ranges from about 0.2 to 0.5. The thickness of CL is tpically around 0.01 mm.
The state of water in the pore regions of CL is the same as in the pore regions of GDL. The water in the ionomer of CL is also the same as in the membrane. The following equaiton can be used to indicate the mass transfer (phase change) direction between the water in the ionomer and the vapor/liquid in the pore regions: \[\lambda_{equil} \begin{cases} =0.043+17.81a-39.85a^2+36.0a^3, & \text{if 0 $\leq$ a $\leq$ 1} \\ =14.0+1.4(a-1), & \text{if 1<a $\leq$ 3} \\ \end{cases}\] where \(\lambda_{equil}\) is the equilibrium water content (the water content in ionomer corresponding to the amount of surrounding water), and a the water activity in the pore regions, defined as \[a=\frac{X_{vp} P_g}{P_{sat}}+2s_{lq}\] where \(X_{vp}\) is the mole fraction of water vapor in the pore regions, \(P_g (Pa)\) the pressure of the gas mixture in the pore regions, \(P_{sat}(Pa)\) the water saturation pressure, and \(s_{lq}\) the liquid water volume fraction in the pore regions.
Here are some reasonable assumptions to simplify the problem. (1) The phase change between the non-frozen membrane water and vapor occurs when non-humidified inlet gases are supplied; (2) only the phase change between the non-frozen membrane water and liquid occurs in cathode CL with fully humidified inlet gases; and (3) only the phase change between the non-frozen membrane water and vapor occurs in anode CL no matter the inlet gases are humidified or not, due to the fact that water is not produced in anode and the EOD effect dries out the anode.

Two important concept: (1) equilibrium water content (\(\lambda_{equil}\)), which indicates the phase change between the non-frozen water in the ionomer of CL and the vapor in the pores of CL; (2) saturation water content (\(\lambda_{sat}\)), which indicates the phase change between the nonfronzen water in the ionomer and the ice in the pores of CL or the forzen water in the membrane layr. The principal driving forces for these water phase change processes in the CL are the differences between the non-frozen water content in the ionomer and the equilibrium water content, and between the non-frozen water content and the saturation water content.

In flow channel

The state of water in ﬂow channel is similar to as in GDL. The chance of ice formation in ﬂow channel is much lower than in CL and GDL, and might be neglected.

Mechanism of water transport

In membrane

The following figure shows the structure of Nafion membrane.

Proton transport

The proton transport has significant influence on the water transport in membrane; on the other hand, the proton transport also largely depends on the membrane environment (water content).
As shown in the figure below, at the low water content, the water in the membrane is mostly likely bound to the slide chains. This kind of water may be classiﬁed as the non-freezable water or surface water. This part of water is strongly bound to the charged sites (\(SO_3^-\)). In this scenario, proton has to transfer through the void volume from one charged site to another.

There are three types of proton transfer mechanism: (1) side chanin viberation; (2) vehicle mechanism (also called the vehicular diffusion); (3) hopping mechanism (also called the Grotthuss mechanism or structure diffusion). (1) If no water is present in a membrane, the proton can only transfer directly between the charged sites (mechanism 1), resulting low proton conductivity. (2) If a little amount of water is present but the amoutn is below the percolation threshold, the proton transfer mechanisms will be mainly the direct transfer between the charged sites (mechanism 1) and vehicle mechanism (mechanism 2). (3) When the amount of water is high than the percolation thrshold, the dominating proton transport mechanisms are the vehicle and hopping mechanisms. It has been reported that for intermediate and low hydration levels, the vehicle mechanism (mechanism 2) stil dominates the proton tranport, and for water content higher than 13, the hopping mechanism (mechanism 3) dominates.
