Material Characterization Technique - XRR

Posted on 2018-10-04 | In Experiment , Research | |

Introduction

X-ray reflectivity (XRR) is a non-destructive method and works for crystalline and amorphous materials. It can measure film thickness from 0.1 nm - 1000 nm.
It is used to analyze X-ray reflection intensity curves from grazing incident X-ray beam to determine thin-film parameters including thickness, density, and surface or interface roughness.

Theory

At x-ray wavelengths, the refractive index of most material is slightly less than one.
$$n=1-\delta-i\beta$$
Typical values of $\delta$ are $10^{-5}$ - $10^{-6}$ and $\beta$ is ten teimes smaller.

When an x-ray beam of intensity $I_0$ hits the interface between two mediums of refractive indexes $n_top$ and $n_bottom$, the beam is partially reflected. The intensity $I$ of the reflected beam is given by the following formula
$$I=I_0 Exp[-\frac{4\pi \mu d}{k_{z,top} \lambda}] \times [r Exp(-2k^2_{z,top} \sigma2)]2$$
where $r$ is Fresnel reflection coefficient, the first exponential factor accounts for the absorption of x-rays with vaccum wavelength $\lambda$ traveling a distance d through the top medium with linear absorption coefficient $\mu$ before hitting the interface. The second exponential factor accounts for diminishing reflectivity at a rough interface with r.m.s roughness $\sigma$.

Data Analysis

When a X-ray beam is impinged at a grazing angle on to an ideal flat surface of a material, total reflection occurs at below the incident angle $\theta_c$, and the incident X-rays do not penetrate into the material. X-ray reflectivity decreases rapidly with increasing incident angle when $\theta$ is above $\theta_c$. The ratio of specularly reflected X-rays decreases proportionally to $\theta^4$.

Film thickness

Figure 4 shows the effect of film thickness on XRR data. Interference occurs between the X-rays reflected from the surface of the Au film and the interface between the Au film and the Si substrate. The oscillations of the reflectivity profile is caused by this X-ray interference. Since these oscillations were first observed in 1931 by Kiessing and thus is called Kiessing fringes. The oscillation depends on the film thickness, and the thicker the film, the shorter period of the oscillations.

Density

Figure 5 shows the effect of film density on XRR data. The amplitude of the oscillation and the critical angel $\theta_c$ for total reflection provide information on the density of films. The amplitude of the oscillation depends on the difference between the densities of the film and its substrate, the larger the difference in the film density, the higher the amplitude of the oscillation.

Surface or interface roughness

Figure 6 indicates that reflected X-rays decrease more rapidly with a larger surface roughness. In other words, the larger the roughness of a film, the faster the decay rate of X-ray reflectivity. Figure 7 suggests that amplitude of the oscillation decreases with increasing interface roughness. The term “interface roughness” includes that physical uneven interface and transitional boundary layer which continually changes in density. In both cases, interface roughness is recognized to a continuous variation of electric density along the thickness direction.
