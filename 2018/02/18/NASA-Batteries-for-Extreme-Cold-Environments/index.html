NASA--Batteries for Extreme Cold Environments

Posted on 2018-02-18 | In Research | |

Requirement

  1. Expected Outcome: Proposals may focus on the development of an enabling component technology (e.g., electrolyte, anode, cathode), but must provide a feasible path to achieving the stated performance metrics based on test data and analysis.
    • Desired Performance Metrics: Operation between -40°C to -80°C
    • Achieving target values over the range of -40⁰C to -80⁰C has the greatest mission relevance; however, demonstration of operation at lower target values down to -80⁰C is still of interest
  2. Targets

    • 300 Wh/kg at C/720 rate (at cell level for primary cells) and >150 Wh/kg at C/5 rate (at cell level for rechargeable cells)

    • 200 Wh/l (at cell level for primary cells) and >150 Wh/l (at cell level for rechargeable cells)

    • Minimum 100 charge/discharge cycles with <5% loss of capacity for rechargeable cells
    • Of particular interest are cells with a nominal voltage of >2.5V for primary cells, and >3.5V for rechargeable cells

Ultra-low Temperature Batteries: A new development in electrolyte chemistry, led by ECS member Shirley Meng, is expanding lithium-ion battery performance, allowing devices to operate at temperatures as low as -60° Celsius.

-40°C and lower

Science: Liquefied Gas Electrolytes for Electrochemical Energy Storage Devices: The use of fluoromethane shows a high coulombic efficiency of ca. 97% for cycling lithium metal anodes, together with good cyclability of a 4 V lithium cobalt oxide cathode and operation as low as -60°C with excellent capacity retention.

  3. Liquid/Solid Phase Diagrams of Binary Carbonates for Lithium Batteries Part II: These two paper provided a complete representation for all the binary combinations of these five common carbonates: EC, PC, DMC, EMC, DEC.
    • The results showed that all the combinations formed simple eutectic systems, although details of their phase diagrams greatly varied. DEC melted at -74.3°C instead of the currently accepted value of -43°C.
    • DEC was less effective than either EMC or DMC in bringing down the liquidus temperatures of their binary mixtures with EC. These results followed the trend we had observed earlier that an expansion toward low temperature of the liquid region of a binary system was facilitated by the two components having close melting points and similar molecular structures.
    • The following phase diagram figure shows different solvents combined with EC. Only DEC has potential to be workable at extremely low temperature.

Jet Propulsion Lab: Li-Ion Electrolytes Containing Ester Co-Solvents for Wide Operating Temperature Range: we have identified a number of electrolyte cosolvents that can be incorporated into multi-component electrolyte formulations for enhanced performance, especially at very low temperatures (down to –70oC).
    • Electrolyte: 1M LiPF6 in EC+EMC+X (20:60:20 v/v %) where X is ester co-solvent, including methyl propionate (MP), ethyl propionate (EP), methyl butyrate (MB), ethyl butyrate (EB), propyl butyrate (PB), and butyl butyrate (BB). with the lower molecular weight, lower viscosity co-solvents generally yielding better performance.

    • To address these mission needs, the Electrochemical Technologies Group (ETG) at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) is engaged in developing ultra-low temperature rechargeable batteries with high specific energy and enhanced low temperature capability for icy moon surface missions. The performance goals of this program include operation over the temperature range of +40oC to -60oC (delivering up to 100 Wh/kg at -40oC and 75 Wh/kg at -60oC).

-40°C or higher

  1. High energy density hybrid Mg2+/Li+ battery with superior ultra-low temperature performance: The designed hybrid battery delivers excellent electrochemical performance at an ultra-low temperature of −40 °C, at which it retains 77% capacity compared to that of room temperature. (With an operating potential of 2.4 V, the hybrid Mg2+/Li+ battery designed by us can deliver a maximum energy density of 382.2 W h kg−1).
  2. Formulation of a New Type of Electrolytes for LiNi1/3Co1/3Mn1/3O2 Cathodes Working in an Ultra-Low Temperature Range: Instead of LiPF6, LiBF4 and LiODFB were used to form this new type of electrolytes, although LiPF6 is the mostly chosen solute in the state-of-the-art Li-ion electrolytes. A LiODFB-based electrolyte demonstrated improved low temperature performance. In particular, at-30°C, a Li-ion cell with 1M LiODFB dissolved in a 1:2:5 (wt.) propylene carbonate (PC)/ethylene carbonate (EC)/ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC) mixed solvent delivered 86% of the capacity obtained at 20°C. Analysis of cell impedance revealed that reduced charge-transfer resistance by using LiODFB resulted in improved low temperature performance of Li-ion batteries.

Diary 2

  1. Organic Batteries Operated at −70°C
    • Pure EA exhibits a low freezing point of -84C. 2 M LiTFSI in EA solution was prepared as the electrolyte for rechargeable batteries at low temperature.
    • It was found that the LIBs based on intercalation compounds cannot work at the ultralow temperature of -70C even with such an electrolyte, which is attributed to the sluggish desolvation of Li+. However, the rechargeable battery taking organic polymers as electrodes can work well at such low temperature and exhibits a capacity retention of ~70% at -70C compared with that at room temperature, owing to the fast kinetics of charge storage on the surface groups or in the large interstitial space of organic solids.
    • Recently, ‘‘allorganic batteries’’ that use organic materials for both the cathode and anode have been attracting extensive attention. This study use polytriphenylamine (PTPAn)- and 1,4,5,8-naphthalenetetracarboxylic dianhydride (NTCDA)-derived polyimide (PNTCDA) as cathode and anode material, respectively. The operation of the rechargeable battery system depends on the reversible absorption/desorption of anions in the cathode and on the reversible enolization of (C=O)n \(\rightleftharpoons\) (C-O-Li)n in the anode.

Summary

Most of batteries are only applicable in the temperature range of -40 to 60°C, such as Ag-Zn batteries (-20 to 25°C), Ni-Cd batteries (-10 to 30°C), Ni-H2 batteries (-10 to 30°C), and Li ion batteries (LIBs) (-20 to 30°C).[1] Among these system, LIBs is much more attractive than aqueous-based chemistries for aerospace applications because of the constraints on the mass and volume of the power system. Therefore, LIBs have been utilized in a number of NASA-JPL missions to Mars and showed excellent performance within temperature range of -20°C to +30°C.[2]

The temperature on Mars is in the range of -99°C to +22°C. To extend the applicable temperature range of LIBs, all the components in a battery are important and can influence the low temperature performance. But, the electrolyte properties have the predominant impact upon low temperature performance, as sufficient electrolyte conductivity is a necessary condition for good performance at low temperatures.[3]

Current literature in developing extremely low temperature electrolytes can be divided into two categories. (1) Reducing the melting points of traditional carbonate electrolytes[3]-[7]; and (2) Searching new electrolyte systems with ultra-low melting points.[8]

Most of commercialized electrolytes for LIBs are binary systems of common carbonates, including ethylene carbonate (EC), propylene carbonate (PC), dimethyl carbonate (DMC), ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), diethyl carbonate (DEC). Among these carbonates, DEC has the lowest melting point (-74.3 °C) followed by EMC (-53°C). The melting points of EC and DMC are as high as 36.4 and 4.6 °C. At extremely low temperature, most of traditional electrolytes would solidify, making battery lose its functionalities. Thus, it is necessarily to know the liquid/solid phase diagram of these binary system. The liquid/solid phase diagram of many common carbonates have been well studied by Michael et al. in Army Research Laboratory.[9],[10] The results suggest that most of the binary mixtures will form solid(s) when temperature is lower than -60 °C, and high portion of DEC has to be utilized to obtain a liquid phase electrolyte at low temperature.

M.C. Smart et al. in Jet Propulsion Laboratory have done lots of work in ultra-low temperature functional batteries by tuning traditional electrolytes since 1990s. The main idea of their work is to add some low melting point component(s) into traditional binary mixture electrolyte so as to reduce the overall melting point of the mixture.[3] For example, after using 1.0 M LiPF6 in EC+DMC+DEC (1:1:1) electrolyte, a AA-size lithium cell was observed to deliver >460 mAh at -58°C at very low rates (C/70 to C/150), which corresponds to >90% of the room temperature capacity.

In 2008, M.C. Smart et al. reported a number of ester co-solvents. All these ester co-solvents have a common feature – low melting point, like methyl propionate (MP, -87.5 °C), ethyl propionate (EP, -73 °C), methyl butyrate (MB, -85.5 °C), ethyl butyrate (EB, -93 °C), propyl butyrate (PB, -95.2 °C), and butyl butyrate (BB, -91.5 °C). One of their results show that the prototype cell containing the 1.0 M LiPF6 EC+EMC+MP (methyl propionate) (20:60:20 v/v %) electrolyte was capable of delivering over 6 times capacity delivered by the cell with the baseline ternary all carbonate blend, and was able to support reasonably aggressive rates at low temperature (-50 and -60 °C). The C/10 discharge capacity at -60°C was over 70% of the nominal capacity at room temperature (5 Ah out of 7 Ah).[6]

In 2018, M. C Smart el al. summary some of their work for Mars missions.[2] The 2003 Mars Exploration Rover (MER) mission uses LIBs with LiNiCoO2 cathodes, meso-carbon microbeads (MCMB) anodes and a ternary carbonate-based electrolyte developed at JPL. One of the Rover is still in operation. The battery was designed to be used in temperature range of -20°C to +30°C, and Radioisotope Heater Units were equipped to keep the temperature since the temperature on Mars is in the range of -99°C to +22°C. The Curiosity Rover landed on the planet Mars in August 06, 2012 also used a Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generator to maintain battery temperature in desired operating range. The Mars Insight mission is scheduled to launch a single lander on Mars in May 2018. Unlike previous Mars rover missions, the Insight mission requires a higher specific energy battery that can operate over a wider temperature range (-30°C to +35°C). To fulfill these stringent requirements, the next generation graphite-LiNiCoAlO2 chemistry coupled with a JPL developed low temperature electrolyte consisting of 1.0M LiPF6 in ethylene carbonate (EC) + ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC) + methyl propionate (MP) (20:60:20) is applied.

There are very few works in searching new electrolyte systems with ultra-low melting points. The first work was proposed by Prof. Y. Shirley Meng’s group in U.C. San Diego in 2017.[8] Their idea is applying liquefied gas as electrolyte for lithium ion battery. Six liquefied gas solvents were studied, and they found the use of fluoromethane shows a high coulombic efficiency of ca. 97% for cycling lithium metal anodes, together with good cyclability of a 4 V.
